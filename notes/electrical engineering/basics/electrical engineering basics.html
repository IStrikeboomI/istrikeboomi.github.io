This section covers the basics of electrical engineering

We will be mainly focusing on circuits

An electrical circuit is a closed path that allows electrical current to flow

Electric circuits are made from components that "process electricity"

And made of shorts/conductors that allow current to move (wires)

What is Electricity?

Electricity is the flow of electrons through a conductor

Electrons have a property called charge in a unit called coulombs

A single electron has a charge of approximately $-1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ coulombs

In other words 1 coulomb is the charge of approximately $6.242 \times 10^{18}$ electrons

Electric current is the flow of charge over time

Current is measured in amperes (amps) where 1 ampere is equal to 1 coulomb per second

Mathematically we can express current as:

$$I(t) = \frac{dQ(t)}{dt}$$

Where $I(t)$ is current in amperes at time $t$, $Q(t)$ is charge in coulombs at time $t$, and $t$ is time in seconds

$\frac{d}{dt}$ represents the derivative (rate of change) in respect to $t$ (time)

If current is constant, then $Q = It$

In other words if a part of a circuit has a current of 1 amp, that means if we split the wire there

And measure the amount of electrons that flow per second, it would be approximately $6.242 \times 10^{18}$


The "force" or pressure that causes current to flow is called voltage (in volts)

Voltage is the electrical potential difference between two points in a circuit

Similar to how a ball high on a ledge has potential energy due to its position

To find the voltage across a component you find the difference between the potential on both sides

Voltage represents potential energy per unit charge

You might have seen the term "volts" in batteries or other power sources


Imagine if I had a circuit with some voltage that causes some current to flow

If I was to increase the voltage (pressure), you would think the current would also increase

This is absolutely correct which represents that current is proportional to voltage

$$ V \propto I$$


Another important property of circuits is resistance (in ohms $\Omega$)

Resistance is a measure of how much a component or material opposes the flow of current

Materials with low resistance allow current to flow easily (conductors)

Materials with high resistance restrict the flow of current (insulators)


Using resistance, we can turn the proportionality into an equation:

$$ V = IR $$

This is known as a very important equation Ohm's Law

We can rearrange this equation to solve for current or resistance if needed:

$$ I = \frac{V}{R} $$

$$ R = \frac{V}{I} $$

Water Analogy

To better understand these concepts, we can use a water analogy

  • Voltage is like water pressure
  • Current is like the flow rate of water
  • Resistance is like a narrow pipe that restricts water flow

Just like how increasing water pressure increases flow rate, increasing voltage increases current

Just like how a narrower pipe restricts water flow, higher resistance restricts current flow

Circuits

Circuits are made up of various components that have different electrical properties

Some common components include:

  • Voltage sources - provide electrical potential difference
  • Resistors - provide resistance to current flow
  • LEDS - emit light when current flows through them
  • Switches - can open or close a circuit to control current flow
  • Potentiometers - variable resistors used to adjust voltage or current
  • Capacitors - store electrical energy temporarily
  • Inductors - store energy in a magnetic field when current flows through them
  • Diodes - allow current to flow in one direction only
  • Transistors - used for switching and amplification of signals

We will be focusing on basic circuits for now so only voltage sources, resistors, and switches

A loop is some path from the positive end of the voltage source (called $V_{cc}$) back to the negative end (called ground)

Applying Ohm's Law In Circuits

Ohm's Law can be applied to analyze simple electrical circuits

For example, consider a circuit with a voltage source and some component with resistance

This is the same circuit as above, just with units filled in

The goal is to find the current through the resistor (same as $I$ but we will talk about that later)

The voltage source is trying to keep the voltage across the resistor at a constant $5$ volts

The resistance of the resistor is $10 \Omega$ (Ohms)

Using Ohm's Law, we can calculate the current $I$ as $I = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{5V}{10 \Omega} = 0.5A$

So there is half an amp through the resistor

Summary

We just went over a lot so let's recap

  • Electricity is the flow of electrons through a conductor
  • Current is the flow of charge (in coulombs) over time (in amperes)
  • Voltage is the electrical potential difference (in volts)
  • Resistance is how much a component opposes current flow (in ohms)
  • Ohm's Law relates voltage, current, and resistance: $V = IR$
  • Circuits are made up of components connected by wires to form closed loops
Practice Problems
  • Find the voltage across a $10K \Omega$ resistor with $100$ mA (milliamps) going through it

    $$\begin{align} 10000 \ \Omega \times 0.1 \ A & = 1000 \ V \\ \end{align}$$

  • Find the current through a $220 \Omega$ resistor with $12V$ across it

    $$\begin{align} I & = \frac{V}{R} \\ I & = \frac{12V}{220 \ \Omega} \\ I & \approx 0.0545 \ A \\ I & \approx 54.5 \ mA \\ \end{align}$$

  • A circuit has a $9$V battery connected to a resistor that is pulling $9$ mA of current. What is the resistance of the resistor?

    $$\begin{align} R & = \frac{V}{I} \\ R & = \frac{9V}{0.009A} \\ R & = 1000 \ \Omega \\ R & = 1 \ k\Omega \\ \end{align}$$
