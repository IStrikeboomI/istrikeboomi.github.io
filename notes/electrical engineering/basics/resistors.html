We discussed resistors earlier in the context of circuits as providing resistance to current

Resistance is measured in ohms (Î©), the higher the resistance the less current it can provide

This is given by Ohm's law $V=IR$

Now resistors are a useful component because they give a known value

But truth is any matter can be used a resistor, because resistance is a property of matter

The way resistors dissipate energy is through heat

The higher the voltage, the higher current it's disspating so the hotter it gets

We can also combine resistance in series or parallel

To combine 2 resistors in series: $R_{total} = R_1 + R_2$

To combine 2 resistors in parallel: $\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}$

To generalize to $n$ resistors

Series:

$$R_{total} = \sum_{i=1}^n R_i = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n$$

Parallel

$$\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{R_i} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}$$

or

$$R_{total} = \left( \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{R_i} \right)^{-1} = \left( \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n} \right)^{-1} $$

Resistivity

Resistivity is a property of matter that indicates how much resistance it provides

Resistivity is measured in ohm meters ($\Omega \cdot m$) and typically denoted with rho $\rho$

Reistance depends on resitivity, length, and also temperature of the material

The resistance of a material is given by the formula

$$R = \rho \frac{L}{A}$$

Where $L$ is the length of the material and $A$ is the cross sectional area

This means that the longer the material, the higher the resistance

And the thicker the material, the lower the resistance

We typically don't worry about resitivity, but with resistance

Since resistivity is a property of matter, everything has resistance

This includes the human body, which has a resistance value that can varies a lot

Typically its between $100k \Omega$ and $1M \Omega$

Types of Resistors

Fixed resistors have a set resistance value, while variable resistors can change their resistance

Variable resistors are also called potentiometers or rheostats

Conductance

If resistance is the opposition to current

Then conductance (in Siemens $\Omega^{-1}$or Mho $\mho$)is the reciprocal of resistance

For example if a resistor has resistance of $50 \Omega$

Then it has a conductance of $\frac{1}{50} \text{ or } .02 \Omega^{-1}$

Resistors in Series And Parallel

For resistors in series:

Using KVL and Ohm's law (the resistors share the same current because they are in series)

$$\begin{align} V_{total} &= V_1 + V_2 \\ &= I R_1 + I R_2 \\ &= I (R_1 + R_2) \\ R_{total} &= R_1 + R_2 \\ \end{align}$$

Combined:

For resistors in parallel:

Using KCL and Ohm's law (the resistors share the same voltage because they are in parallel)

$$\begin{align} I_{total} &= I_1 + I_2 \\ &= \frac{V}{R_1} + \frac{V}{R_2} \\ &= V \left( \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} \right) \\ \frac{1}{R_{total}} &= \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} \\ \end{align}$$

Combined:

If we generalize to $n$ resistors:

Series:

$$R_{total} = \sum_{i=1}^n R_i = R_1 + R_2 + \cdots + R_n$$

Parallel

$$\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{R_i} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{R_n}$$

  • Create a formula that combines $n$ equivalent resistors in parallel

    Since all the resistors are equivalent

    $$\begin{align} \frac{1}{R_{total}} &= \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{R} \\ &= \frac{1}{R} \sum_{i=1}^n 1 &&\text{Since $\frac{1}{R}$ doesn't use $i$} \\ &= n \cdot \frac{1}{R} \\ &= \frac{n}{R} \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} R_{total} &= \frac{R}{n} \\ \end{align}$$

    What you might notice is since $n > 1$, the total resistance decreases as more resistors are added in parallel.
