For a series $ \displaystyle\sum_{n = b}^\infty a_n$, The integral test says

Create a function $f(n) = a_n$

If there is a finite point $k$ where the following is always true after said point on $[k,\infty)$,

  1. $f(n)$ is continuous
  2. $f(n) > 0$ ($f(n)$ is positive)
  3. $f'(n) < 0$ ($f(n)$ is decreasing)

Then if,

$$\begin{align} \int_k^\infty{f(n) \ dn} &\text{ Converges, then } \sum_{n = b}^\infty a_n \text { Converges} \\ \int_k^\infty{f(n) \ dn} &\text{ Diverges, then } \sum_{n = b}^\infty a_n \text { Diverges} \end{align}$$

Why Does This Work?

The core idea behind the integral test is that a definite integral represent a continuous sum

This can be seen in the definition of the definite integral

The series represents a discrete sum

If we graph the function $f(n)$ alongside rectangles with width 1 and height $a_n$ using the left endpoint

We see the total areas of the rectangles is smaller than actual area under the curve (the integral)

This means if the integral converges, then so does the series because the series is less than the integral


We can also apply this when the series overshoots the area (using the right endpoint by doing $a_{n+1}$)

Here the area of the rectangles is larger than the actual area under the curve

So if the integral diverges, so does the series because the series is larger than the integral


How do we know if its under or over estimate?

We can do both, but one of them is gonna give one of the following the conclusions

  1. The series is larger than the convergent integral
  2. The series is smaller than the divergent integral

Both of these statements are essentially useless

Do we have to start the integral at $k$?

You don't! you can have any finite lower bound $N \geq 1$ because it doesn't effect the convergence/diverge of the integral

Example

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 2}^\infty {\frac{1}{n\ln(n)}} \end{align}$$

First, we need to verify the conditions that we can use the integral test

$$\begin{align} f(n) &= \frac{1}{n\ln(n)} \\ f'(n) &= -\frac{\ln(n) + 1}{(n\ln(n))^2} \\ \end{align}$$

Since the denominator is always positive, the numerator is the only one affecting the sign

$$\begin{align} f'(n) &< 0 \\ -\ln(n) + 1 &< 0 \\ -\ln(n) &< -1 \\ \ln(n) &> 1 &&\text{Flip Inequality}\\ n &> e\\ \end{align}$$

So $f(n)$ is decreasing after $e$, and is clearly positive and continuous in the interval $[e,\infty)$

This means we can apply the integral test

Let's take the integral

$$\begin{align} \int_e^\infty{\frac{1}{n\ln(n)} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_e^t{\frac{1}{n\ln(n)} \ dn} \\ u = \ln(n) &, du = 1/n \ dn \\ n \to e &, u \to 1 \\ n \to t &, u \to \ln(t) \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_1^{\ln(t)}{\frac{1}{u} \ du} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[\ln(|u|)\right]_1^{\ln(t)} \\ &= \infty \\ \end{align}$$

So this series diverges

Practice Problems
  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty {\frac{1}{n}}$

    $$\begin{align} f(n) &= \frac{1}{n} \\ f'(n) &= -\frac{1}{n^2} \\ f'(n) &< 0 &\text{ For all } n \end{align}$$

    $f(n)$ is clearly decreasing, continuous, and positive from $[1,\infty)$

    $$\begin{align} \int_1^\infty{\frac{1}{n} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_1^t{\frac{1}{n} \ dn} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\left[\ln(|n|)\right]_1^t \\ &= \infty\\ \end{align}$$

    So this series diverges by the integral test

  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty {\frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2}}$

    $$\begin{align} f(n) &= \frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2} \\ f'(n) &= -\frac{e^{1/n}(1 + 2x)}{n^4} \\ f'(n) &< 0 &\text{ For all } n \in [1,\infty) \end{align}$$

    $f(n)$ is clearly decreasing, continuous, and positive from $[1,\infty)$

    $$\begin{align} \int_1^\infty{\frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_1^t{\frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2} \ dn} \\ u = 1/n &, du = -1/n^2 \ dn \rightarrow -du = 1/n^2 \ dn\\ n \to 1 &, u \to 1 \\ n \to t &, u \to 1/t \\ &= -\lim_{t \to \infty}\int_1^{1/t}{e^{u} \ du} \\ &= -\lim_{t \to \infty}\left[e^u\right]_1^{1/t} \\ &= -(e^0 - e^1) \\ &= e - 1 \\ \end{align}$$

    So this series converges by the integral test

  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty {n^2e^{-n}}$

    $$\begin{align} f(n) &= n^2e^{-n} \\ f'(n) &= 2ne^{-n} - n^2e^{-n} \\ f'(n) &< 0 \\ 2ne^{-n} - n^2e^{-n} &< 0 \\ (ne^{-n})(2 - n) &< 0 \\ 2 - n &< 0 &&\text{$ne^{-n}$ is always positive}\\ n &> 2 \\ \end{align}$$

    $f(n)$ is clearly decreasing, continuous, and positive from $[2,\infty)$

    $$\begin{align} \int_1^\infty{n^2e^{-n} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_1^t{n^2e^{-n} \ dn} \\ \end{align}$$

    Using the tabular method,

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c | c} S & D & I \\ \hline + & n^2 & e^{-n}\\ - & 2n & -e^{-n}\\ + & 2 & e^{-n}\\ - & 0 & -e^{-n}\\ \end{array} \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_1^t{n^2e^{-n} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\left[-n^2e^{-n} - 2ne^{-n} - 2e^{-n}\right]_1^t\\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\left[-t^2e^{-t} - 2ne^{-t} - 2e^{-t} - (1^2e^{-1} - 2(1)e^{-1} - 2e^{-1})\right]_1^t\\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}-t^2e^{-t}- 2ne^{-t} - 2e^{-t} - (-1^2e^{-1}- 2(1)e^{-1} - 2e^{-1})\\ &= 0 + 0 + 0 - \left(\frac{1}{e} - \frac{2}{e} - \frac{2}{e}\right)\\ &= \frac{3}{e}\\ \end{align}$$

    So this series converges by the integral test
