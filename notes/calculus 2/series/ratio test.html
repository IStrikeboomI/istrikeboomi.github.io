The Ratio Test for a series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^\infty{a_n}$ states:

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|} = L \\ \end{align}$$

If $L < 1$, the series converges absolutely (it converges)

If $L > 1$, the series diverges

If $L = 1$, the test is inconclusive

The ratio test is best applied with series involving factorials or exponentials

Examples:

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n=1}^\infty{\frac{n3^n}{n!}} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)3^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \cdot \frac{n!}{n3^n}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)3^{n}3}{(n+1)n!} \cdot \frac{n!}{n3^n}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{3}{n}\right|} = 0\\ \end{align}$$

Since $0 < 1$, this series converges absolutely

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n=1}^\infty{\frac{n^n}{n!}} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \cdot \frac{n!}{n^n}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)^{n}(n+1)}{(n+1)n^n}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)^{n}}{n^n}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left(\frac{n+1}{n}\right)^n} &&\text{Inner Limit Is Always Positive}\\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left(n + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n} = e &&\text{Limit Defintion of $e$}\\ \end{align}$$

Since $e > 1$, this series diverges

Proof

Let:

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|} = L \\ \end{align}$$

If $L < 1$:

Take $r$ such that $L < r < 1$

For large $n \geq N$ (because the limit is going to $\infty$): $\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| < r < 1$

$$\begin{align} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| < r \rightarrow \left|a_{n+1}\right| < r|a_n| \\ \left|a_{N+1}\right| < r|a_N| &&\tag{1} \\ \left|a_{N+2}\right| < r|a_{N+1}| < r(r|a_n|) &&\text{By (1)} \\ \left|a_{N+2}\right| < r|a_{N+1}| < r^2|a_n| \end{align}$$

We can generalize this:

$$\begin{align} \left|a_{N+k}\right| &< r^k|a_N| \\ \sum_{i = b}^\infty{\left|a_{N+k}\right|} &< \sum_{i = b}^\infty{r^k|a_N|} \\ \end{align}$$

We know the right series is a geometric series, so it converges if $r < 1$ (which was established earlier)

And by using direct comparison test, the series on the right also converges

We can rewrite the series on the right into our original series by splitting the series

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{|a_n|} = \sum_{n = 1}^N{\left|a_{n}\right|} + \sum_{n = N+1}^\infty{\left|a_{n}\right|} \end{align}$$

So this series is convergent, which makes our original series absolutely convergent


If $L > 1$:

$$\begin{align} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| > 1 \rightarrow |a_{n+1}| > |a_n| \end{align}$$

This means the sequence is increasing, which means the series cannot converge by the test for divergence

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}|a_n| \neq 0 \rightarrow \lim_{n \to \infty}a_n \neq 0 \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Determine Convergence Or Divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n 3^n}{2^n + 1}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n 3^n}{2^n + 1}} \end{align}$$

    Since we are taking the absolute value, we just ignore the $(-1)^n$

    $$\begin{align} &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{3^{n+1}}{2^{n+1} + 1} \cdot \frac{2^n + 1}{3^n}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{3(2^n + 1)}{2^{n+1} + 1}\right|} \\ &3\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{2^n + 1}{2^{n+1} + 1}\right|} \\ &3\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{1 + 1/2^n}{2 + 1/2^n}\right|} \\ &3\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{3}{2}\\ \end{align}$$

    Since $3/2 > 1$, this series diverges

  • Determine If You Can Apply The Ratio Test for $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2 \cdot 4^n}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(2n)!}{(n!)^2 \cdot 4^n}} \end{align}$$

    Since we are taking the absolute value, we just ignore the $(-1)^n$

    $$\begin{align} &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(2(n+1))!}{((n+1)!)^2 \cdot 4^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{(n!)^2 \cdot 4^n}{(2n)!}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(2n+2)!}{((n+1)n!)^2 \cdot 4^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{(n!)^2 \cdot 4^n}{(2n)!}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(2n+2)(2n+1)(2n!)}{(n+1)^2n!^2 \cdot 4^{n}4} \cdot \frac{(n!)^2 \cdot 4^n}{(2n)!}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(2n+2)(2n+1)}{(n+1)^2 \cdot 4}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{4n^2 + 6n + 2}{(n^2 + 2n + 1)\cdot 4}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{4n^2 + 6n + 2}{4n^2 + 8n + 4}\right|} = 1\\ \end{align}$$

    Since $1 = 1$, you cannot apply the ratio test

  • Determine Convergence Or Divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n n^2}{2^n}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n=0}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n n^2}{2^n}} \end{align}$$

    Since we are taking the absolute value, we just ignore the $(-1)^n$

    $$\begin{align} &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{2^{n + 1}} \cdot \frac{2^n}{n^2}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{(n^2)2}\right|} \\ &\lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{n^2 + 2n + 1}{2n^2}\right|} = \frac{1}{2}\\ \end{align}$$

    Since $1/2 < 1$, this series converges
