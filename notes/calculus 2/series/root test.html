The Root Test (or Cauchy's n'th root/radical test) for a series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^\infty{a_n}$ states:

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}} = \lim_{n \to \infty}{|a_n|^{1/n}} = L \\ \end{align}$$

If $L < 1$, the series converges absolutely (it converges)

If $L > 1$, the series diverges

If $L = 1$, the test is inconclusive

The root test is best applied with series involving exponentials

Examples

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n=1}^\infty{\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^{n}} \\ \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)^{n}\right|^{1/n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{1}{n}}\\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

And since $0 < 1$, this series converges absolutely, so it converges


$$\begin{align} \sum_{n=3}^\infty{\left(2 + \frac{4}{n}\right)^{n}} \\ \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\left(2 + \frac{4}{n}\right)^{n}\right|^{1/n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{2 + \frac{4}{n}}\\ &= 2 \end{align}$$

And since $2 > 1$, this series diverges

Proof

This proof is very similar to the one in the ratio test

Let:

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\sqrt[n]{|a_n|}} = L \\ \end{align}$$

If $L > 1$:

Take $r$ such that $L < r < 1$

For large $n \geq N$ (because the limit is going to $\infty$): $\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} < r < 1$

$$\begin{align} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} < r \rightarrow |a_n| < r^n \\ \sum_{n = k}^\infty{|a_n|} < \sum_{n = k}^\infty{r^n} \end{align}$$

We know the right series is a geometric series, so it converges if $r < 1$ (which was established earlier)

And by using direct comparison test, the series on the right also converges

We can rewrite the series on the right into our original series by splitting the series

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{|a_n|} = \sum_{n = 1}^N{\left|a_{n}\right|} + \sum_{n = N+1}^\infty{\left|a_{n}\right|} \end{align}$$

So this series is convergent, which makes our original series absolutely convergent


If $L > 1$:

$$\begin{align} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} > 1 \rightarrow |a_n| > 1 \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}|a_n| \neq 0 \rightarrow \lim_{n \to \infty}a_n \neq 0 \end{align}$$

Which means the series diverges by the Test For Divergence

Practice Problems
  • Determine Convergence Or Divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty{\left(\frac{-2n}{6n + \sin(n)}\right)^{2n}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n=1}^\infty{\left(\frac{-2n}{6n + \sin(n)}\right)^{2n}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\left(\frac{-2n}{6n + \sin(n)}\right)^{2n}\right|^{1/n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left(\frac{2n}{6n + \sin(n)}\right)^{2}} \\ &= \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^{2} \\ &= \frac{1}{9} < 1 \\ \end{align}$$

    So this series converges

  • Determine Convergence Or Divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty{\frac{n^n}{e^{n^2}}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n=1}^\infty{\frac{n^n}{e^{n^2}}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\frac{n^n}{e^{n^2}}\right|^{1/n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{n}{e^{n}}} \\ &= 0 < 1 \\ \end{align}$$

    So this series converges

  • Determine Convergence Or Divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty{\left(1 + \frac{e\sqrt{\pi}}{n}\right)^{n^2}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n=1}^\infty{\left(1 + \frac{e\sqrt{\pi}}{n}\right)^{n^2}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left|\left(1 + \frac{e\sqrt{\pi}}{n}\right)^{n^2}\right|^{1/n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\left(1 + \frac{e\sqrt{\pi}}{n}\right)^{n}} \\ &= e^{e\sqrt{\pi}} > 1 &&\text{By Limit Definition of $e$}\\ \end{align}$$

    So this series diverges
