The Series Comparison Tests are the Direct Comparison Test and the Limit Comparison Test

Direct Comparison Test (DCT):

If $a_n$ and $b_n$ are both positive sequences after some point $k$,

And $a_n < b_n$ after all $n$ after some point $k$, then:

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = k}^\infty{a_n} &\text{ Converges if } \sum_{n = k}^\infty{b_n} \text{ Converges} \\ \sum_{n = k}^\infty{b_n} &\text{ Diverges if } \sum_{n = k}^\infty{a_n} \text{ Diverges} \end{align}$$

Limit Comparison Test (LCT):

If $a_n$ and $b_n$ are both positive sequences for all $n$ after some point $k$,

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = L \end{align}$$

If $L$ is finite and positive, $0 < L < \infty$, then both series either converge or diverge

When choosing a series to compare with, it is common and easiest to choose a Geometric Series or P-Series

You can find it by using the largest growing terms in the denominator and numerator

What you might notice is these tests are very to similar to the improper integral comparison tests

An important thing to remember for the DCT, if you divide by a larger amount, it is a smaller result and vice-verse

Why These Works

For the direct comparison test:

If one sequence is always larger than other, and the other sequence diverges to $\infty$

That means the other sequence is larger than $\infty$, which means it has to diverge to $\infty$

Similarly, if the sequence is smaller and the other converges

Smaller than a convergent value is still a convergent value

If we were larger than a convergent value, that could also be convergent or it could be $\infty$

If we were smaller than a divergent value, that could also be convergent or it could be $\infty$


For the limit comparison test:

Recall what happens at limits at infinty

If the denominator is faster growing, the limit is 0, which tells us nothing

If the numerator is faster growing, the limit is $\infty$, which tells us nothing

If the they both grow at the same rate, the limit is a finite number, because they grow proportionallly ($a_n \propto b_n$)

Which means they must have the same end behavior so they must both diverge or converge

Examples

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2 + 1}} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} n^2 + 1 &> n \\ \frac{1}{n^2 + 1} &< \frac{1}{n} \\ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2 + 1}} &< \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2 }} \end{align}$$

Since the series on the right is p-series with $p = 2$, so it converges

Which means this series converges by the DCT


$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2 - 1}} \end{align}$$

Take

$$\begin{align} b_n = \frac{1}{n^2} \end{align}$$

Which is a convergent p-series

If we apply LCT:

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{e^{1/n}}{n^2 - 1} \cdot n^2} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{n^2e^{1/n}}{n^2 - 1}} \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{e^{1/n}}{1 - 1/n^2}} \\ &= \frac{e^0}{1 - 0} \\ &= 1 \end{align}$$

And since $1 > 0$, the LCT shows this series converges

Practice Problems
  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2 + 5n}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2 + 5n}} \end{align}$$

    Compare this with the convergent p-series:

    $$\begin{align} b = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{n^2}{n^2 + 5n}} = 1 \end{align}$$

    Since $1 > 0$, this series converges by the LCT

  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{4^n + n^2}{5^n - 2n}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{4^n + n^2}{5^n - 2n}} \end{align}$$

    Compare this with the convergent geometric series:

    $$\begin{align} b = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{4^n}{5^n}} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\left(\frac{4}{5}\right)^n} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{4^n + n^2}{5^n - 2n} \cdot \frac{5^n}{4^n}} &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{5^n(4^n + n^2)}{4^n(5^n - 2n)}} \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{5^n(4^n)(1 + n^2/4^n)}{4^n(5^n)(1 - 2n/5^n)}} \\ &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{1 + n^2/4^n}{1 - 2n/5^n}} \\ &= \frac{1 + 0}{1 - 0}\\ &= 1 \end{align}$$

    Since $1 > 0$, this series converges by the LCT

  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{5^n}{2^n + 3^n + 4^n}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{5^n}{2^n + 3^n + 4^n}} \end{align}$$

    Compare this with the divergent geometric series:

    $$\begin{align} 2^n + 3^n + 4^n &< 4^n + 4^n + 4^n \\ \frac{5^n}{2^n + 3^n + 4^n} &> \frac{5^n}{4^n + 4^n + 4^n} \\ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{5^n}{2^n + 3^n + 4^n}} &> \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{5^n}{3(4^n)}} \\ \end{align}$$

    Since the series on the right is a divergent geometric-series, the original series diverges

  • Determine the convergence or divergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{e^{-n\ln(n)}}$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{e^{-n\ln(n)}} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{e^{n\ln(n)}}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \ln(n) &> 1 \\ n\ln(n) &> n \\ e^{n\ln(n)} &> e^n \\ \frac{1}{e^{n\ln(n)}} &< \frac{1}{e^n} \\ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{e^{n\ln(n)}}} &< \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{e^n}} \\ \end{align}$$

    The series on the right is a geometric series

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{e^n}} &= \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\left(\frac{1}{e}\right)^n}\\ \end{align}$$

    And since $1/e < 1$, it makes the geomtric series convergent, which makes the original series convergent

  • Show that $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{(n - a)^p}}$ converges under the same conditions of the p-series test ($a$ and $p$ are constants)

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{(n - a)^p}} \end{align}$$

    Take $b$ as

    $$\begin{align} b = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^p}} \end{align}$$

    Which only converges using the conditions of the p-series

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to 1}{\frac{n^p}{(n-a)^p}} = 1 \end{align}$$

    Since $1 > 0$, this series converges by the LCT
