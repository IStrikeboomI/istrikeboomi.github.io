A Series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^\infty{a_n}$ is Absolutely Convergent if:

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n=k}^\infty{|a_n|} &\text{ Converges} \end{align}$$

A Series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^\infty{a_n}$ is Conditionally Convergent if the following are both true:

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n=k}^\infty{a_n} &\text{ Converges}\\ \sum_{n=k}^\infty{|a_n|} &\text{ Diverges} \end{align}$$


If a series is absolutely convergent, it is also convergent, the inverse however is not always true


Riemann series theorem (or Riemann Rearrangement theorem):

If a series is absolutely convergent, the terms can be rearranged and give the same sum value

If a series is condtionally convergent, the terms can be rearranged to give any sum value

Using Absolute Convergence To Prove Convergence

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{\sin(n)}{2^n}} \end{align}$$

We can't use DCT or LCT because the series has negative results

However, we can take the absolute value and show that this series is absolutely convergent

$$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\left|\frac{\sin(n)}{2^n}\right|} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{|\sin(n)|}{2^n}} &&\text{Because $2^n > 0$} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} |\sin(n)| &\leq 1 \\ \frac{|\sin(n)|}{2^n} &\leq \frac{1}{2^n} \\ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty\frac{|\sin(n)|}{2^n} &\leq \sum_{n = 1}^\infty\frac{1}{2^n} \\ \end{align}$$

And Since $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}{\frac{1}{2^n}}$ is a convergent geometric series, $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty\frac{|\sin(n)|}{2^n}$ converges by the DCT

But since the series is absolutely convergent, that means the original series is convergent

Proof Of Convergence

$$\begin{align} a_n &\leq |a_n| \\ 0 < |a_n| + a_n &\leq 2|a_n| \\ \sum_{n = k}^\infty{(|a_n| + a_n)} &\leq 2\sum_{n = k}^\infty{|a_n}| \\ \sum_{n = k}^\infty{|a_n|} + \sum_{n = k}^\infty{a_n} &\leq 2\sum_{n = k}^\infty{|a_n}| \\ \end{align}$$

Since $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^\infty{|a_n|}$ converges (given) that means that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=k}^\infty{a_n}$ HAS to converge to keep the inequality valid

Practice Problems
  • Determine if $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{(-1)^n \frac{\ln(n)}{n}}$ is absolutely convergent, condtionally convergent, or diverges

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{(-1)^n \frac{\ln(n)}{n}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\left|(-1)^n \frac{\ln(n)}{n}\right|} &= \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{\ln(n)}{n}} \\ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{\ln(n)}{n}} &\geq \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n}} \end{align}$$

    Since $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n}}$ is a divergent p-series, the absolute series diverges

    This means we need to move on and check the original series

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{(-1)^n \frac{\ln(n)}{n}} \end{align}$$

    I'm going to use AST because all the conditions fit

    $$\begin{align} f(n) &= \frac{\ln(n)}{n} \\ f'(n) &= \frac{1 - \ln(n)}{n^2} \\ 1 - \ln(n) &< 0 \\ ln(n) &> 1 \\ n &> e \rightarrow n > 3 \text{ Terms} \end{align}$$

    So $f(n)$ is decreasing after 3 terms and it is always postive

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}{\frac{\ln(n)}{n}} = 0 \end{align}$$

    So the original series converges by the AST, but it is not absolutely convergent

    This means it is Conditionally Convergent

  • Determine if $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{\cos(n)}{n^2}}$ is absolutely convergent, condtionally convergent, or diverges

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{\cos(n)}{n^2}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\left|\frac{\cos(n)}{n^2}\right|} = \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{|\cos(n)|}{n^2}} \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} |\cos(n)| &\leq 1 \\ \frac{|\cos(n)|}{n^2} &\leq \frac{1}{n^2} \\ \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{|\cos(n)|}{n^2}} &\leq \sum_{n = 1}^\infty{\frac{1}{n^2}} \\ \end{align}$$

    Since the series on the right is a convergent p-series, that means the series converges by the DCT

    This makes the original series Absolutely Convergent, which also makes it convergent

  • Determine if $\displaystyle\sum_{n = 2}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))}}$ is absolutely convergent, condtionally convergent, or diverges

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 2}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 2}^\infty{\left|\frac{(-1)^n}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))}\right|} = \sum_{n = 2}^\infty{\frac{1}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))}} \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} f(n) = \frac{1}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))} \end{align}$$

    Here $f(n)$ is decreasing, positive, and continous for $n > 2$

    This means we can apply the Integral Test

    $$\begin{align} \int_2^\infty{\frac{1}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_2^t{\frac{1}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))} \ dn} \\ u = \ln(ln(n)) &, du = \frac{1}{n\ln(n)} \ dn \\ n \to 2 &, u \to \ln(\ln(2)) \\ n \to t &, u \to \ln(\ln(t)) \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_{\ln(\ln(2))}^{\ln(\ln(t))}{\frac{1}{u} \ du} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\left[\ln(|u|)\right]_{\ln(\ln(2))}^{\ln(\ln(t))} \\ &= \infty \\ \end{align}$$

    Since the absolute series diverges, we need to find the convergence of the original series

    $$\begin{align} \sum_{n = 2}^\infty{\frac{(-1)^n}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))}} \end{align}$$

    Using AST

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{n\to \infty}{\frac{1}{n\ln(n)\ln(\ln(n))}} = 0 \end{align}$$

    So the original series converges, but not the absolute series

    Which makes this series Conditionally Convergent
