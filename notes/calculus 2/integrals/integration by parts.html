Integration by parts is an integration technique that can be thought of as the inverse of the product rule for derivatives

For Indefinite Integrals:

$$\begin{align} \int{u \ dv} = uv - \int{v \ du} \end{align}$$

OR

$$\begin{align} \int{f(x)g'(x)dx} = f(x)g(x) - \int{g(x)f'(x) \ dx} \end{align}$$

For Definite Integrals:

$$\begin{align} \int_a^b{u \ dv} = uv - \int_a^b{v \ du} \end{align}$$

OR

$$\begin{align} \int_a^b{f(x)g'(x)dx} = f(x)g(x) - \int_a^b{g(x)f'(x) \ dx} \end{align}$$

You have to be careful when choosing $u$ and $dv$

For choosing $u$ choose which is easier to take the derivative of to get $du$

For choosing $dv$ choose which is easier to take the anti-derivative of to get $v$

There is a common acronym to choosing your $u$ value called LIATE

Choose your $u$ if the above order

If your initial choices fail, find another $u$ and $dv$

Where Does This Come From?

First write out the product rule

$$\begin{align} \left[f(x)g(x)\right]' = f'(x)g(x) + g'(x)f(x) \end{align}$$

Take Integral of both sides

$$\begin{align} \int{\left[f(x)g(x)\right]' \ dx} &= \int{f'(x)g(x) + g'(x)f(x) \ dx} \\ f(x)g(x) &= \int{f'(x)g(x) \ dx} + \int{g'(x)f(x) \ dx} \\ \end{align}$$

Finally Rearrange

$$\begin{align} \int{f(x)g'(x) \ dx} = f(x)g(x) - \int{g(x)f'(x) \ dx} \end{align}$$

Examples

Example 1:

$$\begin{align} &\int{x^3 ln(x) \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = ln(x) & v = \frac{x^4}{4} \\ \hline du = \frac{1}{x} & dv = x^3 \end{array} \\ &ln(x) \cdot \frac{x^4}{4} - \int{\frac{x^4}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \ dx} \\ &\frac{x^4ln(x)}{4} - \int{\frac{x^4}{4x}\ dx} \\ &\frac{x^4ln(x)}{4} - \frac{1}{4}\int{x^3\ dx} \\ &\frac{x^4ln(x)}{4} - \frac{1}{4}\int{x^3\ dx} \\ &\frac{x^4ln(x)}{4} - \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{x^4}{4}\\ \int{x^3 ln(x) \ dx} &= \frac{x^4ln(x)}{4} - \frac{x^4}{16} + C\\ \end{align}$$

Example 2:

$$\begin{align} &\int{x^3 e^x \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = x^3 & v = e^x \\ \hline du = 3x^2 & dv = e^x \end{array} \\ &x^3e^x - \int{3x^2e^x \ dx} \\ &x^3e^x - 3\int{x^2e^x \ dx} \end{align}$$

Here we get another integral where we need to repeat integration by parts

$$\begin{align} &x^3e^x - 3\int{x^2e^x \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = x^2 & v = e^x \\ \hline du = 2x & dv = e^x \end{array} \\ &x^3e^x - 3(x^2e^x - \int{2xe^x \ dx}) \\ &x^3e^x - 3x^2e^x - 6\int{xe^x \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = x & v = e^x \\ \hline du = 1 & dv = e^x \end{array} \\ &x^3e^x - 3x^2e^x - 6(xe^x - \int{e^x dx}) \\ \int{x^3 e^x \ dx} &= x^3e^x - 3x^2e^x - 6xe^x - 6e^x + C\\ \end{align}$$

Example 3:

$$\begin{align} &\int{e^{2x}sin(3x) \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = e^{2x} & v = -\frac{cos(3x)}{3} \\ \hline du = 2e^{2x} & dv = sin(3x) \end{array} \\ &-\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2}{3}\int{e^{2x}cos(3x) \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = e^{2x} & v = \frac{sin(3x)}{3} \\ \hline du = 2e^{2x} & dv = cos(3x) \end{array} \\ &-\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2}{3}\left(\frac{sin(3x)e^{2x}}{3} - \frac{1}{3}\int{e^2xsin(3x) \ dx}\right) \\ &-\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2sin(3x)e^{2x}}{9} - \frac{2}{9}\int{e^2xsin(3x) \ dx} \\ \end{align}$$

If we keep doing integration by parts we are going to end up in an endless loop, so we need to do something else

Notice how the integral in our current solution is our original integral

Also notice how the original integral is equal to the whole current solution

$$\begin{align} \int{e^{2x}sin(3x) \ dx} &= -\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2sin(3x)e^{2x}}{9} - \frac{2}{9}\int{e^2xsin(3x) \ dx} \\ \frac{9}{9}\int{e^{2x}sin(3x) \ dx} &= -\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2sin(3x)e^{2x}}{9} - \frac{2}{9}\int{e^2xsin(3x) \ dx} \\ \frac{11}{9}\int{e^{2x}sin(3x) \ dx} &= -\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2sin(3x)e^{2x}}{9}\\ \int{e^{2x}sin(3x) \ dx} &= \frac{9}{11}\left[-\frac{cos(3x)e^{2x}}{3} + \frac{2sin(3x)e^{2x}}{9}\right] + C\\ \end{align}$$
