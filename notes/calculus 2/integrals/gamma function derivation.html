The gamma function is defined as:

$$\begin{align} \Gamma(z) = \int_0^{\infty}{e^{-t}t^{z - 1} \ dt} \end{align}$$

This is commonly used to represent factorials of real numbers

$$\begin{align} \Gamma(z) = (z - 1)! \end{align}$$

There is also a related function called the Pi Function or the Gauss Pi Function

The Capital Pi Function is just the gamma function shifted by one

$$\begin{align} \Pi(z) = \Gamma(z + 1) = \int_0^{\infty}{e^{-t}t^{z} \ dt} = z! \end{align}$$

This function looks really weird especially since it relates to factorials, this is how to derive it

This is also how we will expand factorials from integers to the real and complex numbers

Factorial Rules

First we need to set up some base rules for factorials so we can use it to derive a better function

  1. $0! = 1$
  2. $1! = 1$
  3. $n! = n \cdot (n-1)!$
  4. Factorials of negative integers are undefined

Integral of Powers of $ln(x)$

Let's first try to find the answer to the following integral using integration by parts

$$\begin{align} &\int{ln(x) \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = ln(x) & v = x \\ \hline du = \frac{1}{x} & dv = 1 \ dx \end{array} \\ \\ \int{ln(x) \ dx} &= x \cdot ln(x) - \int{\frac{1}{x} \cdot x \ dx} \\ &= xln(x) - x + C \end{align}$$

Now lets find the answer to a similar integral

$$\begin{align} &\int{(ln(x))^2 \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = (ln(x))^2 & v = x \\ \hline du = \frac{2ln(x)}{x} & dv = 1 \ dx \end{array} \\ \\ \int{(ln(x))^2 \ dx} &= x \cdot (ln(x))^2 - \int{\frac{2ln(x)}{x} \cdot x \ dx} \\ &= x(ln(x))^2 - 2\int{ln(x) \ dx} \\ &= x(ln(x))^2 - 2(xln(x) - x) \\ &= x(ln(x))^2 - 2xln(x) + 2x + C \\ \end{align}$$

Let's repeat this again with 3 instead of 2

$$\begin{align} &\int{(ln(x))^3 \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = (ln(x))^3 & v = x \\ \hline du = \frac{3ln(x)^2}{x} & dv = 1 \ dx \end{array} \\ \\ \int{(ln(x))^3 \ dx} &= x \cdot (ln(x))^3 - \int{\frac{3ln(x)^2}{x} \cdot x \ dx} \\ &= x(ln(x))^3 - 3\int{ln(x)^2\ dx} \\ &= x(ln(x))^3 - 3(x(ln(x))^2 - 2xln(x) + 2x) \\ &= x(ln(x))^3 - 3x(ln(x))^2 + 6xln(x) - 6x + C\\ \end{align}$$

Let's generalize this to a power $n$

$$\begin{align} &\int{(ln(x))^n \ dx} \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} u = (ln(x))^n & v = x \\ \hline du = \frac{nln(x)^{n-1}}{x} & dv = 1 \ dx \end{array} \\ \\ \int{(ln(x))^n \ dx} &= x \cdot (ln(x))^n - \int{\frac{nln(x)^{n-1}}{x} \cdot x \ dx} \\ &= x(ln(x))^n - n\int{ln(x)^{n-1} \ dx} \end{align}$$

Let's use this to extend to more integers

$$\begin{align} \int{(ln(x))^4 \ dx} &= x(ln(x))^4 - 4(x(ln(x))^3 - 3x(ln(x))^2 + 6xln(x) - 6x) \\ &= x(ln(x))^4 - 4x(ln(x))^3 + 12x(ln(x))^2 - 24xln(x) + 24x + C\\ \int{(ln(x))^5 \ dx} &= x(ln(x))^5 - 5(x(ln(x))^4 - 4x(ln(x))^3 + 12x(ln(x))^2 - 24xln(x) + 24x) \\ &= x(ln(x))^5 - 5x(ln(x))^4 + 20x(ln(x))^3 - 60x(ln(x))^2 + 120xln(x) - 120x + C\\ \end{align}$$

Solving A Limit

Later on we need to try to solve the general limit of the first part of the integral

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0^+} {x(ln(x)^n)} = 0 &&\tag{1} \end{align}$$

We need to use induction so solve base case ($n = 1$)

To solve this we can use L'Hopital's Rule

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0^+} {xln(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{ln(x)}{1/x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{\frac{d}{dx} ln(x)}{\frac{d}{dx} 1/x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{\frac{1}{x}}{-\frac{1}{x^2}}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} -x \\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

Assume limit (1) as true

Now to solve the general limit we can try to solve for $n + 1$

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0^+} {x(ln(x)^{n + 1})} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{ln(x)^{n + 1}}{1/x}} \\ \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{x}{1/(ln(x)^{n + 1})}} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{\frac{d}{dx} ln(x)^{n + 1}}{ \frac{d}{dx} 1/x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {\frac{(n + 1) \cdot ln(x)^{n} \cdot 1/x}{-1/x^2}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {-x^2 \cdot (n + 1) \cdot ln(x)^{n} \cdot \frac{1}{x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {-x^2 \cdot (n + 1) \cdot ln(x)^{n} \cdot \frac{1}{x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} {-x \cdot (n + 1) \cdot ln(x)^{n}} \\ &= -(n + 1)\lim_{x \to 0^+} {xln(x)^{n}} \\ &= -(n + 1)\cdot 0 &&\text{Assume (1) as true} \\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

This goes back to our original limit answer proving it true

Moving Over To a Definite Integral

If you notice the the last term in the integral sequence where it's just a coefficient with x

The coefficient can be extracted into a factorial term by $n$

$$\begin{align} -1, 2, -6, 24, -120 \end{align}$$

The negative is easy to deal we can use multiply by $(-1)^n$ to cancel out the negative

$$\begin{align} (-1)^n n! \end{align}$$

We need to try to extract just the coefficient of the last term, the way we can do this is to plug in 1

For example for $n = 5$

$$\begin{align} f(x) &= \int{(ln(x))^5 \ dx} = x(ln(x))^5 - 5x(ln(x))^4 + 20x(ln(x))^3 - 60x(ln(x))^2 + 120xln(x) - 120x \\ f(1) &= 1(ln(1))^5 - 5(1)(ln(1))^4 + 20(1)(ln((1)))^3 - 60(1)(ln((1)))^2 + 120(1)ln(1) - 120(1) \\ &= -120 \end{align}$$

We can get an answer involving factorials by using a definite integral where the upperbound is 1

Now we need to find a lower bound

We need this lower bound to be 0 when we plug it in

So what we can we plug in for 0, well we just found a limit for it earlier, we can use 0

We have to use a limit because $ln(x)$ is not defined at $x = 0$

$$\begin{align} \int_0^1{ln(x)^n \ dx} &= \left[ x(ln(x))^n + nx(ln(x))^{n-1} + ... + (-1)^n n!x \right] \biggr\vert_0^1 \\ &= (-1)^n n! - \lim_{x \to 0}{ \left[x(ln(x))^n + nx(ln(x))^{n-1} +... + (-1)^n n!x\right]} \\ &= (-1)^n n! - 0 &&\text{Using Limit (1)} \\ \end{align}$$

This relates our factorials to our integrals

$$\begin{align} (-1)^n n! = \int_0^1{ln(x)^n \ dx}\\ \end{align}$$

Simplifying Our Integral

$$\begin{align} (-1)^n n! &= \int_0^1{ln(x)^n \ dx}\\ n! &= \frac{1}{(-1)^n}\int_0^1{ln(x)^n \ dx}\\ &= \int_0^1{\frac{(ln(x))^n}{(-1)^n} \ dx}\\ &= \int_0^1{(-ln(x))^n \ dx} &&\text{Using Exponent Quotient Property}\\ &= \int_0^1{ln(x^{-1})^n \ dx} &&\text{Using Logarithim Power Property}\\ &= \int_0^1{ln\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^n \ dx}\\ \end{align}$$

Now we can use u-sub, but we will use the variable $t$

$$\begin{align} n! &= \int_0^1{ln\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^n \ dx}\\ t = ln\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) &\rightarrow dt = \frac{-1/x^2}{1/x}dx = -\frac{1}{x}dx \rightarrow -xdt = dx\\ e^t = \frac{1}{x} &\rightarrow e^{-t} = x \\ x = 0 &\rightarrow t = \infty \\ x = 1 &\rightarrow t = 0 \\ n! &= \int_{\infty}^0{-xt^n \ dt} \\ &= \int_{\infty}^0{-e^{-t} t^n \ dt} &&\text{Because $x = e^{-t}$}\\ &= -\int_{\infty}^0{e^{-t} t^n \ dt} \\ &= \int_0^{\infty}{e^{-t} t^n \ dt} \\ \end{align}$$

Now we got our Pi function!

$$\begin{align} n! = \Pi(n) &= \int_0^{\infty}{e^{-t} t^n \ dt} \\ \end{align}$$
