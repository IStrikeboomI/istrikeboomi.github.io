The Arc Length of a function $y = f(x)$ on $[a,b]$ is:

$$\begin{align} \int_a^b{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2} \ dx} \end{align}$$

For a function $x = g(x)$ on $[c,d]$

$$\begin{align} \int_c^d{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dx}{dy}\right)^2} \ dx} \end{align}$$

Where Does This Come From?

We will be using a similar technique to the getting the definition of the definite integral

What our process will be is to split the arc length into line segments, then add up all the line lengths

We have the following graph of $f(x)$

What we will do is make $n$ line segments, each with a width of $\Delta x$

As you see as $n$ increases, the lines become more accurate to the true graph

For the rest I will use the $n = 5$ graph, but the principle still applies to

We will denote the start point of the line as $(x_i,y_i)$, and the end point as $(x_{i + 1},y_{i + 1})$

Let's denote the length of the $i$'th line as $D_i$

We can use the distance formula (which is just the pythagorean theorem)

$$\begin{align} D_i = \sqrt{(x_{i+1} - x_i)^2 + (y_{i+1} - y_i)^2} \end{align}$$

Since $\Delta x$ is the x distance between the lines, we can replace it

We also can denote the $i$'th y-distance as $\Delta y_i$

$$\begin{align} \Delta x &= x_{i+1} - x_i \\ \Delta y_i &= y_{i+1} - y_i \\ D_i &= \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y_i^2} \end{align}$$

We can replace $\Delta y_i$ using the Mean Value Theorem

We know there is a point $c \in [x_i,x_{i+1}]$

$$\begin{align} (y_{i+1} - y_i) &= f'(c)(x_{i+1} - x_i) \\ \Delta y_i &= f'(c)\Delta x \\ D_i &= \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + (f'(c)\Delta x)^2} \\ D_i &= \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + f'(c)^2\Delta x^2} \\ D_i &= \sqrt{(\Delta x^2)(1 + f'(c)^2)} \\ D_i &= \Delta x\sqrt{1 + f'(c)^2} \\ \end{align}$$

Now we can sum the distances together

$$\begin{align} \sum_{i = 1}^n{\sqrt{1 + f'(c)^2}\Delta x} \end{align}$$

Since the lines come closer to the curve, the more lines we have, we take a limit to infinity

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty}\sum_{i = 1}^n{\sqrt{1 + f'(c)^2}\Delta x} \end{align}$$

This looks a lot like the definition of the Definite Integral

$$\begin{align} \int_a^b{\sqrt{1 + \left(f'(x)\right)^2} \ dx} \end{align}$$

Replacing $f'(x)$ with $dy/dx$

$$\begin{align} \int_a^b{\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2} \ dx} \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Find the arc length of $y=\sqrt{x^2 - x} + \sin^{-1}(\sqrt{x})$ on $[1/2,1]$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{dy}{dx} &= \sqrt{\frac{1 - x}{x}} \\ 1 + \frac{dy}{dx}^2 &= 1 + \frac{1 - x}{x} \\ &= \frac{x}{x} + \frac{1 - x}{x} \\ &= \frac{1}{x} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \int_{1/2}^{1}{\sqrt{\frac{1}{x}} \ dx} \\ \int_{1/2}^{1}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \ dx} \\ \int_{1/2}^{1}{x^{-1/2} \ dx} \\ \left[2\sqrt{x}\right]\Big|_{1/2}^{1} \\ 2 - 2 \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\ 2 - \sqrt{2} \end{align}$$

  • Find the arc length of $y=\ln(\sec(x))$ on $[0,\pi/6]$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{dy}{dx} &= \tan(x) \\ 1 + \frac{dy}{dx}^2 &= 1 + \tan^2(x) \\ &= \sec^2(x) \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \int_{0}^{\pi/6}{\sqrt{\sec^2{x}} \ dx} \\ \int_{0}^{\pi/6}{|\sec{x}|\ dx} \\ \int_{0}^{\pi/6}{\sec{x} \ dx} &&\text{Since $\sec(x)$ is always positive in the range}\\ \left[\ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)|\right]\Big|_{0}^{\pi/6}\\ \ln|\sec(\pi/6) + \tan(\pi/6)| - \ln|\sec(0) + \tan(0)| \\ \ln|\frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}| - \ln|1 + 0| \\ \ln|\frac{3}{\sqrt{3}}|\\ \end{align}$$
