Improper integrals deal with definite integrals where one or both of the bounds approach infinity or a discontinuity

Improper integrals are solved by replacing the infinity/discontinuity by a limit

A type I improper integral goes to infinity

$$\begin{align} \int_{a}^{\infty} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_{a}^{t} f(x) \ dx \\ \int_{-\infty}^{a} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\int_{t}^{a} f(x) \ dx \\ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\int_{t}^{a} f(x) \ dx + \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_{a}^{t} f(x) \ dx \\ \end{align}$$

A type II improper integral goes to a discontinuity (or horizontal asymptote)

Discontinuity at $d$

$$\begin{align} \int_{a}^{d} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to d^-}\int_{a}^{d} f(x) \ dx \\ \int_{d}^{a} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to d^+}\int_{d}^{a} f(x) \ dx \\ \end{align}$$

An improper integral converges if it results in a finite value

An improper integral diverges if it results in $\pm \infty$ or $DNE$ (limit does not exist)

Graphically

A type I improper integral is just the area under the curve all the way to infinity

This can (sometimes) work because the function decreases to 0 fast enough to where the area goes to a single value

However sometimes, an integral doesn't go to 0, or doesn't decrease slow enough, so the area under is infinite

All of the following areas below diverge

The way we would represent these as integrals are

$$\begin{align} \int_0^\infty{\frac{x + \cos(x)}{x} \ dx} &= \infty \\ \int_0^\infty{\frac{1}{x} \ dx} &= \infty \\ \int_0^\infty{\frac{x^3}{4} \ dx} &= \infty \\ \end{align}$$


For type II improper integral, its the same as type I but it approaches a horizontal asymptote instead of a vertical asymptote

The same convergence and divergence issues can occur

The following integral converges:

$$\begin{align} \int_1^2{\ln(x-1) \ dx} = -1 \end{align}$$

The following integrals diverge:

$$\begin{align} \int_0^1{\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \ dx} &= \infty \\ \int_1^3{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} &= \infty \end{align}$$

Solving Improper Integrals

The core idea behind solving Improper Integrals is to replace the infinity/discontinuity with a new placeholder variable

Then you wrap the integral in a limit where the placeholder varaible goes to your infinity/discontinuity

For example:

$$\begin{align} \int_1^\infty{\frac{1}{x^2} \ dx} &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_1^t{\frac{1}{x^2} \ dx} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[-\frac{1}{x} \right]_1^t &&\text{Using Power Rule}\\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[-\frac{1}{t} - -\frac{1}{1} \right] &&\text{Using FTC}\\ &= 0 + 1\\ &= 1\\ \end{align}$$


$$\begin{align} \int_2^\infty{\frac{1}{x\ln(x)} \ dx} &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_2^t{\frac{1}{x\ln(x)} \ dx} \\ u = \ln(x) &, du = \frac{1}{x} \ dx \\ \text{as } x \to 2 &, u \to \ln(2) \\ \text{as } x \to t &, u \to \ln(t) \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \int_{\ln(2)}^{\ln(t)}{\frac{1}{u} \ dx} &&\text{U-sub}\\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \left[\ln|u|\right]_{\ln(2)}^{\ln(t)} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty} \ln|\ln(t)| - \ln|\ln(2)| \\ &= \infty \\ \end{align}$$

For an integral approaching a discontinuity, don't forget to make it a sided limit

If the discontinuity is the lower bound, it approaches from the right ($d^+$)

If the discontinuity is the upper bound, it approaches from the left (d^-)

Let's solve the example from above

$$\begin{align} \int_1^2{\ln(x-1) \ dx} &= \lim_{t \to 1^+}\int_t^2{\ln(x-1) \ dx} \\ u = x-1 &, du = dx \\ x \to 2 &, u \to 1 \\ x \to t &, u \to t-1 \\ &= \lim_{t \to 1^+}\int_{t-1}^1{\ln(u) \ du}\\ &= \lim_{t \to 1^+}\left(\left[u\ln(u)\right]_{t-1}^1 - \int_{t-1}^1{\frac{u}{u}\ du}\right) &&\text{Integration by Parts} \\ &= \lim_{t \to 1^+}\left(\left[u\ln(u)\right]_{t-1}^1 - \left[u\right]_{t-1}^1\right) \\ &= \lim_{t \to 1^+}\left[u\ln(u) - u\right]_{t-1}^1 \\ &= \lim_{t \to 1^+} 1ln(1) - 1 - (t-1)ln(t-1) + (t-1) \\ &= 0 - 1 - 0\ln(2) + 0 \\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

If there is a discontinuity in the middle of the bounds, or the integral goes from $-\infty$ to $\infty$, you need to split the integral into two

You need to split at a common point $a$ where it exists in both functions, typically this value is 0, but it can be anything

$$\begin{align} \int_{a}^{b} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to c^-}\int_{a}^{c} f(x) \ dx + \lim_{t \to c^+}\int_{c}^{b} f(x) \ dx \\ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \ dx &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\int_{t}^{a} f(x) \ dx + \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_{a}^{t} f(x) \ dx \\ \end{align}$$

For the example above:

$$\begin{align} \int_1^3{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} &= \int_1^2{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} + \int_2^3{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} \\ &= \lim_{t \to 2^-}\int_1^t{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} + \lim_{t \to 2^+}\int_t^3{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} \\ &= \lim_{t \to 2^-}\left[-\frac{3}{x-2}\right]_1^t+ \lim_{t \to 2^+}\int_t^3{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} \\ &= \infty+ \lim_{t \to 2^+}\int_t^3{\frac{3}{(x - 2)^2} \ dx} \\ &= \infty \end{align}$$

Since one of the integrals diverged, it doesn't matter if the other integral converges because infinity plus any number will still be infinty

This let's us only need to compute one of the integrals if it goes to infinity because we can know that it already diverges

Practice Problems
  • $\int_{-\infty}^0{\frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \ dx}$

    $$\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^0{\frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \ dx} &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\int_{t}^0{\frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \ dx} \\ &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\left[\tan^{-1}(x)\right]_t^0 \\ &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\tan^{-1}(t)- \tan^{-1}(0) \\ &= \frac{\pi}{2} - 0 \\ &= \frac{\pi}{2} \\ \end{align}$$

  • $\int_{0}^{\infty}{e^{-n} \ dn}$

    $$\begin{align} \int_{0}^{\infty}{e^{-n} \ dn} &= \lim_{t \to 0}\int_{0}^{t}{e^{-n} \ dn} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\left[-e^{-n}\right]_0^t \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}-e^{0} + \frac{1}{e^t}\\ &= -1 + 0\\ &= -1\\ \end{align}$$

  • $\int_{1}^{\infty}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \ dx}$

    $$\begin{align} \int_{1}^{\infty}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \ dx} &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\int_{1}^{t}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{x}} \ dx} \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}\left[2\sqrt{x}\right]_1^t \\ &= \lim_{t \to \infty}2\sqrt{t} - 2\sqrt{1} \\ &= \infty - 2 \\ &= \infty\\ \end{align}$$

    This integral diverges

  • $\int_{-1}^{0}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}} \ dt}$

    $$\begin{align} \int_{-1}^{0}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}} \ dt} &= \lim_{n \to -1^+}\int_{n}^{0}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-t^2}} \ dt} \\ &= \lim_{n \to -1^+}\left[\sin^{-1}(t)\right]_n^0 \\ &= \lim_{n \to -1^+}\sin^{-1}(0) - \sin^{-1}(n) \\ &= 0 - -\frac{\pi}{2}\\ &=\frac{\pi}{2}\\ \end{align}$$

  • $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}{\frac{x^2}{x^3 - 1} \ dx}$

    Note there is a discontinuity at $x = 1$ so we need to do a nested limit

    $$\begin{align} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}{\frac{x^2}{x^3 - 1} \ dx} &= \int_{-\infty}^{1}{\frac{x^2}{x^3 - 1} \ dx} + \int_{1}^{\infty}{\frac{x^2}{x^3 - 1} \ dx}\\ &= \lim_{t \to -\infty}\lim_{n \to 1^-}\int_{t}^{n}{\frac{x^2}{x^3 - 1} \ dx} + \lim_{t \to \infty}\lim_{n \to 1^+}\int_{n}^{t}{\frac{x^2}{x^3 - 1} \ dx}\\ u = x^3 - 1 &, du = 3x^2 \ dx \rightarrow \frac{du}{3} = x^2 \ dx \\ x \to t &, u \to t^3 - 1 \\ x \to n &, u \to n^3 - 1 \\ &= \frac{1}{3}\left(\lim_{t \to -\infty}\lim_{n \to 1^-}\int_{t^3 - 1}^{n^3 - 1}{\frac{1}{u} \ du} + \lim_{t \to \infty}\lim_{n \to 1^+}\int_{n^3 - 1}^{t^3 - 1}{\frac{1}{u} \ du}\right)\\ &= \frac{1}{3}\left(\lim_{t \to -\infty}\lim_{n \to 1^-}\left[\ln(|u|)\right]_{t^3 - 1}^{n^3 - 1} + \lim_{t \to \infty}\lim_{n \to 1^+}\left[\ln(|u|)\right]_{n^3 - 1}^{t^3 - 1}\right)\\ &= \frac{1}{3}\left(\lim_{t \to -\infty}\lim_{n \to 1^-}\left[\ln(|n^3 - 1|) - \ln(|t^3 - 1|)\right] + \lim_{t \to \infty}\lim_{n \to 1^+}\left[\ln(|t^3 - 1|) - \ln(|n^3 - 1|)\right]\right)\\ &= \frac{1}{3}\left(\lim_{t \to -\infty}\lim_{n \to 1^-}\left[-DNE - \ln(|t^3 - 1|)\right] + \lim_{t \to \infty}\lim_{n \to 1^+}\left[\ln(|t^3 - 1|) - -\infty)\right]\right)\\ \end{align}$$

    Since the limit Does not exist, it will diverge
