Trig sub is a method to solve certain integrals using a u-sub into trig functions, then using trig properties to simplyfy

When you see the follwing, do the corresponding substitution

$$\begin{align} \sqrt{x^2 - a^2} &\rightarrow x = a\sec(\theta), dx = a\sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta \\ \sqrt{a^2 - x^2} &\rightarrow x = a\sin(\theta), dx = a\cos(\theta) \ d\theta \\ \sqrt{x^2 + a^2} &\rightarrow x = a\tan(\theta), dx = a\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta \\ \end{align}$$

At the end, convert $\theta$ to $x$ by using similar techniques to simplyfying trig expressions

Why Use Trig Sub?

It makes integrals involving the square root easier

When you do the substitution, the part inside the square root can be factored out

You can factor the $a$ out of the square root and convert whats left into the following trig indentities

$$\begin{align} \sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1 \\ \tan^2(\theta) + 1 = \sec^2(\theta) \end{align}$$

Example

$$\begin{align} &\int{\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}{x^2} \ dx} \\ &x = 2\sin(\theta), dx = 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta \\ &\int{\frac{\sqrt{4 - (2\sin(\theta))^2}}{(2\sin(\theta))^2} 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{\sqrt{4 - 4\sin^2(\theta)}}{4\sin^2(\theta)} 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{\sqrt{4(1 - \sin^2(\theta))}}{4\sin^2(\theta)} 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{2\sqrt{1 - \sin^2(\theta)}}{4\sin^2(\theta)} 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{2\sqrt{\cos^2(\theta)}}{4\sin^2(\theta)} 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{2\cos(\theta)}{4\sin^2(\theta)} 2\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{4\cos^2(\theta)}{4\sin^2(\theta)} \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\cot^2(\theta)\ d\theta} \\ &\int{(\csc^2(\theta) - 1)\ d\theta} \\ &-\cot(\theta) - \theta\\ \end{align}$$

We are not done yet, because we are using $\theta$, not $x$

Since $\sin(\theta) = x/2$, this means a triangle with an opposite of $x$, hypotenuse of $2$, and adjacent of $\sqrt{4 - x^2}$

This means $\cot(\theta) = \sqrt{4 - x^2}/x$ (because cotangent is adjacent over opposite)

And $\theta = \sin^{-1}(x/2)$

Subbing these back into the integral

$$\begin{align} \int{\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}{x^2} \ dx} = -\frac{\sqrt{4 - x^2}}{x} - \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right) + C \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Solve $\int{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}/x \ dx}$

    $$\begin{align} I &= \int{\frac{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}}{x} \ dx} \\ &= \int{\frac{\sqrt{25}\sqrt{x^2 - \frac{4}{25}}}{x} \ dx} \\ &= 5\int{\frac{\sqrt{x^2 - \frac{2^2}{5^2}}}{x} \ dx} \\ &x = \frac{2}{5} \sec(\theta), dx = \frac{2}{5} \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta \\ &= 5\int{\frac{\sqrt{(\frac{2}{5} \sec(\theta))^2 - \frac{2^2}{5^2}}}{\frac{2}{5} \sec(\theta)} \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{\frac{\sqrt{\frac{2^2}{5^2} \sec^2(\theta) - \frac{2^2}{5^2}}}{\frac{2}{5} \sec(\theta)} \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{\frac{\frac{2}{5}\sqrt{\sec^2(\theta) - 1}}{\frac{2}{5} \sec(\theta)} \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{\frac{\sqrt{\tan^2(\theta)}}{\sec(\theta)} \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{\frac{\tan(\theta)}{\sec(\theta)} \sec(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{\tan(\theta)\tan(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{\tan^2(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 5\int{(\sec^2(\theta) - 1)\ d\theta} \\ &= 5(\tan(\theta) - \theta) \\ \end{align}$$

    Since $\sec(\theta) = \frac{5x}{2}$, this means we have a triangle with hypotenuse of $5x$, adjacent of $2$, and opposite of $\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}$

    This means that tangent is $\tan(\theta) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}}$ and $\theta = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}}\right)$

    $$\begin{align} \int{\frac{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}}{x} \ dx} = 5\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}} - \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{25x^2 - 4}}\right)\right) + C\\ \end{align}$$

  • Solve $\int{(9 + x^2)^{-1/2}\ dx}$

    $$\begin{align} &\int{(9 + x^2)^{-1/2}\ dx} \\ &\int{\frac{1}{\sqrt{9 + x^2}}\ dx} \\ &x=3\tan(\theta), dx = 3\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta \\ &\int{\frac{1}{\sqrt{9 + (3\tan(\theta))^2}} 3\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{1}{\sqrt{9 + 9\tan^2(\theta)}} 3\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{1}{3\sqrt{1 + \tan^2(\theta)}} 3\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{1}{\sqrt{\sec^2(\theta)}}\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\frac{1}{\sec(\theta)}\sec^2(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\int{\sec(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &\ln|\sec(\theta) + \tan(\theta)| \\ \end{align}$$

    Since $tan(\theta) = \frac{x}{3}$, this gives us a triangle of opposite $x$, adjacent $3$, and hypotenuse $\sqrt{x^2 + 9}$

    This gives $\sec(\theta) = \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 9}}{3}$

    $$\begin{align} &\int{\frac{1}{\sqrt{9 + x^2}}\ dx} = \ln \left|\frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 9}}{3} + \frac{x}{3}\right| + C\\ \end{align}$$

  • Using $y = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$ is the equation for a semi-circle (half circle), use trig sub to derive the circle area formula ($A = \pi r^2$)

    $$\begin{align} f(x) = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2} \end{align}$$

    I am going to integrate from $0$ to $r$ to get the area of half the semi-circle (so a fourth of a full circle)

    $$\begin{align} A = 4\int_0^r{\sqrt{r^2 - x^2} \ dx} \\ x = r\sin(\theta) \rightarrow \theta = \sin^{-1}{\frac{x}{r}}, dx = r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta \\ x=0 \rightarrow \theta = 0, x=r \rightarrow \theta = \pi/2 \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} A &= 4\int_0^{\pi/2}{\sqrt{r^2 - (r\sin(\theta))^2} r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 4\int_0^{\pi/2}{\sqrt{r^2 - r^2\sin^2(\theta)} r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 4\int_0^{\pi/2}{\sqrt{r^2(1 - \sin^2(\theta))} r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 4\int_0^{\pi/2}{r\sqrt{1 - \sin^2(\theta)} r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 4\int_0^{\pi/2}{r\sqrt{\cos^2(\theta)} r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 4\int_0^{\pi/2}{r\cos(\theta) r\cos(\theta) \ d\theta} \\ &= 4r^2\int_0^{\pi/2}{\frac{1}{2} + \frac{\cos(2\theta)}{2}\ d\theta} &&\text{Rearranged $\cos(2\theta) = 2\cos^2(\theta) - 1$}\\ &= 4r^2 \left[\frac{\theta}{2} + \frac{\sin(2\theta)}{4}\right] \Biggr|_0^{\pi/2} \\ &= 4r^2 \left[\frac{\pi/2}{2} + \frac{\sin(2\pi/2)}{4} - \frac{0}{2} - \frac{\sin(0)}{4}\right] \\ &= 4r^2 \left[\frac{\pi}{4} + 0 - 0 - 0\right] \\ &= 4r^2\frac{\pi}{4} \\ &= \pi r^2\\ \end{align}$$
