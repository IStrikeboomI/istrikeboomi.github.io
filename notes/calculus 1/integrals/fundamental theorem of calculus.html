The Fundamental Theorem Of Calculus (FTC) Comes in 2 parts

For the following, assume $f(x)$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F(x)$ is the anti-derivative of f(x)

Part 1:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \int_a^x{f(t) \ dt} = f(x) \end{align}$$

Part 2:

$$\begin{align} F(x) &= \int{f(x) \ dx}\\ \int_a^b{f(x) \ dx} &= \left[F(x)\right] \Big|_a^b = F(b) - F(a) \end{align}$$

Part 1 is about how derivatives and integrals are inverses

Part 2 is about using indefinite integrals to solve definite integrals

Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dt}\int_6^t{x^{x^x} \ dx}$

    We just simply use part 1 of FTC

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dt}\int_6^t{x^{x^x} \ dx} = t^{t^t} \end{align}$$

  • The anti-derivative of $f(x)$ is $sin(x^3)$, find $\int_0^{\sqrt[3]{\pi / 4}}{f(x) \ dx}$

    We just simply use part 2 of FTC

    $$\begin{align} \int_0^{\sqrt[3]{\pi / 4}}{f(x) \ dx} = sin(\pi / 4) - sin(0) = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}\int_{4}^{\sqrt{x}}{e^{sin(t)} \cdot ln(t) \ dt}$

    We just simply use part 1 of FTC

    The indefinite integral would be

    $$\begin{align} F(t) = \int{e^{sin(t)} \cdot ln(t) \ dt} \end{align}$$

    And the indefinite integral would be

    $$\begin{align} \int_{4}^{\sqrt{x}}{e^{sin(t)} \cdot ln(t) \ dt} = F(\sqrt{x}) - F(4) \end{align}$$

    Taking the derivative of this

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} F(\sqrt{x}) - F(4) = (e^{sin(\sqrt{x})}\cdot ln(\sqrt{x})) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} &&\text{Using Chain Rule} \end{align}$$

Proofs
  • Part 1

    Let's define a function $g(x)$ which is the integral of $f(x)$

    Our goal is to show $g'(x) = f(x)$

    $$\begin{align} g(x) &= \int_a^x{f(t) \ dt} \\ g'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(x + h) - g(x)}{h} &&\text{Using Limit Definition Of Derivative} \end{align}$$

    We can replace the $g$ with integrals

    $$\begin{align} g'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\int_a^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} - \int_a^x{f(t) \ dt}}{h} \\ g'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\int_a^{x}{f(t) \ dt} + \int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} - \int_a^x{f(t) \ dt} }{h} &&\text{Splitting definite integral bounds}\\ g'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt}}{h} \\ g'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} \\ \end{align}$$

    To solve this limit, we need to use the squeeze theorem

    We first need to use the extreme value theorem ($f(x)$ is continuous so we can use it)

    We can denote the min and max x-values on $[x,x+h]$ as $u$ and $v$ respectively, the y-values can be $m$ and $M$ respectively

    $$\begin{align} f(u) = m &&\text{Min} \\ f(v) = M &&\text{Max} \\ \end{align}$$

    Since $u$ and $v$ are the smallest and largest values in the bounds, that means $f(t)$ is between them

    $$\begin{align} f(u) \leq f(t) \leq f(v) \end{align}$$

    Since the definite integral of f(t) is the area, we need to make the other 2 into areas to compare them

    $f(u)(x + h - x)$ will be the area of rectangle the length of h and height of the min, this will be smaller than the integral

    $f(v)(x + h - x)$ will be the area of rectangle the length of h and height of the max, this will be larger than the integral

    $$\begin{align} f(u)h \leq \int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} \leq f(v)h \\ f(u) \leq \frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} \leq f(v) \\ \end{align}$$

    Now we can apply squeeze theorem

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{h \to 0}f(u) \leq \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} \leq \lim_{h \to 0}f(v) \\ \end{align}$$

    The limits for the min and max are simple enough because $u$ and $v$ are in the range $[x, x+h]$

    This means as $h \to 0$ the range becomes $[x,x]$, so both limits are equal to $f(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{h \to 0}f(u) &= f(x) \\ \lim_{h \to 0}f(v) &= f(x)\\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} f(x) \leq \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} \leq f(x) \\ \end{align}$$

    This means the limit is equal to f(x), which means the derivative is equal to f(x)

    $$\begin{align} g'(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h}{f(t) \ dt} = f(x)\\ \end{align}$$

  • Part 2

    Let's say again that we have two different anti-derivatives for a function $f(x)$ named $g(x)$ and $F(x)$

    Define $g(x)$ as

    $$\begin{align} g(x) = \int_a^x{f(x) \ dx} \end{align}$$

    Since they are both anti-derivatives of the same function, they are different by some constant $C$

    $$\begin{align} F(x) = g(x) + C \end{align}$$

    Since $F(x)$ and $g(x)$ are differentiable (their derivatives equal $f(x)$), they are continous

    $$\begin{align} g(a) &= \int_a^a{f(x) \ dx} = 0 \\ g(b) &= \int_a^b{f(x) \ dx} \\ \end{align}$$

    Therefore,

    $$\begin{align} F(b) - F(a) = (g(b) + C) - (g(a) + C) = g(b) - g(a) = g(b) = \int_a^b{f(x) \ dx} \end{align}$$
