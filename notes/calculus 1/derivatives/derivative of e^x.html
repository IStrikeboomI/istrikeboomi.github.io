The derivative of $e^x$ is

$$\frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x$$

This means that $e^x$ is it's own derivative!

Proof

Definition of $e \approx 2.71828$:

$$\begin{align} e &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{{(1+\frac{1}{n})}^n} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}{{(1+h)}^{1/h}} &&\text{let $h = \frac{1}{n}$, as $n \rightarrow \infty, h \rightarrow 0$ } \label{e def} \tag{1} \end{align}$$

Derivative:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}e^x &= \lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{e^{x+h} - e^{x}}{h}} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{e^{x}e^{h} - e^{x}}{h}} &&\text{Exponent Property } a^{m+n} = a^ma^n \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{e^{x}(e^{h} - 1)}{h}} \\ &= e^x\lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{e^{h} - 1}{h}} &&\text{The $e^x$ is not being affected by the limit (it can be factored out)} \\ &= e^x\lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{{({(1+h)}^{1/h})}^{h} - 1}{h}} &&\text{by (1)} \\ &= e^x\lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{(1+h) - 1}{h}} \\ &= e^x\lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{h}{h}} \\ &= e^x(1) \\ &= e^x \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dx}e^{3x}$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}e^{3x} = 3e^{3x} &&\text{Using Chain Rule} \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}xe^{x}$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}xe^{x} &= (1)(e^x) + (x)(e^x) &&\text{Using Product Rule} \\ &= e^x + xe^x \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}\frac{cos(e^x)}{2e^x}$

    $$\begin{align} &\text{We need to use Quotient Rule} \\ \\ &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = cos(e^x) & g(x) = 2e^x \\ \hline f'(x) = -e^xsin(e^x) & g'(x) = 2e^x \end{array} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx}\frac{cos(e^x)}{2e^x} &= \frac{(2e^x)(-e^xsin(e^x)) - (cos(e^x))(2e^x)) }{(2e^x)^2} &&\text{Using Quotient Rule} \\ &= \frac{-2(e^x)^2sin(e^x) - 2e^xcos(e^x)}{4(e^x)^2} \\ &= \frac{(-2e^x)(e^xsin(e^x)+cos(e^x)}{4(e^x)^2} \\ &= \frac{-(e^xsin(e^x) + cos(e^x))}{2e^x} \end{align}$$
