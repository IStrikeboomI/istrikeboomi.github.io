So we know how to get the slope of the tangent line, using derivatives

But how do we ge the full equation?

We use point-slope form!

Just a recap of point-slope form, if we have a point $(x_1,y_1)$ and a slope $m$

$$\begin{align} y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \end{align}$$

So if we want the slope at a point on $f(x)$ at $x = c$, we know our point is $(c,f(c))$ and the slope is $f'(c)$

$$\begin{align} y - f(c) = f'(c)(x - c) \end{align}$$

Or,

$$\begin{align} y = f'(c)x - f'(c)c + f(c) \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Find the equation of the tangent line at $x = 2$ for $f(x) = 4x + 3$

    $$\begin{align} f'(x) &= 4 \\ f'(2) &= 4 \\ f(2) &= 11 \\ \end{align}$$

    We can plug these into point-slope

    $$\begin{align} y &= 4(x - 2) + 11 \\ y &= 4x - 8 + 11 \\ y &= 4x + 3 \end{align}$$

  • Find the equation of the tangent line at $x = \pi$ for $f(x) = \sin(x) + 2\\cos(x)$

    $$\begin{align} f'(x) &= \cos(x) - 2\sin(x) \\ f'(\pi) &= -1 \\ f(\pi) &= -2 \\ \end{align}$$

    We can plug these into point-slope

    $$\begin{align} y &= -1(x - \pi) - 2 \\ y &= -x + \pi - 2 \\ \end{align}$$

  • Find the equation of the tangent line at $x = \sqrt{\pi}$ for $f(x) = sin^5(x^2)$

    To find the derivative, we need to use the chain rule twice

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^5 & g(x) = \sin(x^2) \\ \hline f'(x) = 5x^4 & g'(x) = ? \end{array} \end{align}$$

    We need to use chain rule again to find the derivative of $sin(x^2)$

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sin(x) & g(x) = x^2 \\ \hline f'(x) = \cos(x) & g'(x) = 2x \end{array} \end{align}$$

    This gives us $2x\cos(x^2)$, which we can plug back into the top table

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^5 & g(x) = sin(x^2) \\ \hline f'(x) = 5x^4 & g'(x) = 2xcos(x^2) \end{array} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} f'(x) &= 5sin^4(x^2) \cdot 2xcos(x^2) = 10xsin^4(x^2)\cos(x^2) \\ f'(\sqrt{\pi}) &= 10(\sqrt{\pi})(0)(-1) = 0\\ f(\sqrt{\pi}) &= 0 \\ \end{align}$$

    We can plug these into point-slope

    $$\begin{align} y &= 0 \end{align}$$
