Logarithmic differentiation is a technique that utilizes the property of logarithms to make solving difficult derivatives easier

Look at the following function

$$f(x) = \frac{x^4\sqrt{x+2}}{3^x(x^2 + 1)}$$

This would be a difficult derivative to solve using just previous rules

Instead, we could take the natural log of both sides and use logarithim rules to solve each part

Here are the following logarithim rules:

$$\begin{align} ln(ab) &= ln(a) + ln(b) \tag{1} \\ ln(\frac{a}{b}) &= ln(a) - ln(b) \tag{2}\\ ln(a^b) &= b \cdot ln(a) \tag{3} \end{align}$$

And then using the derivative of logarithim

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} ln(u) = \frac{u'}{u} \tag{4} \\ \text{Where $u$ is a function of $x$} \end{align}$$

So lets try to simplyify the function above

$$\begin{align} f(x) &= \frac{x^4\sqrt{x+2}}{3^x(x^2 + 1)} \\ ln(f(x)) &= ln(\frac{x^4\sqrt{x+2}}{3^x(x^2 + 1)}) \\ ln(f(x)) &= ln(x^4\sqrt{x+2}) - ln({3^x(x^2 + 1)}) &&\text{Using (1)}\\ ln(f(x)) &= ln(x^4) + ln(\sqrt{x+2}) - (ln(3^x) + ln(x^2 + 1)) &&\text{Using (2)}\\ ln(f(x)) &= 4ln(x) + \frac{1}{2}ln(x+2) - (xln(3) + ln(x^2 + 1)) &&\text{Using (3)}\\ \end{align}$$

This simplified function is much easier to differentiate because we can use the sum rule for derivative to split it

Also, another easy way to use log rules for a fraction, if it's in the numerator the log is being added, if it's in the denominator the log is being subtracted

$$\begin{align} ln(f(x)) &= 4ln(x) + \frac{1}{2}ln(x+2) - xln(3) - ln(x^2 + 1) \\ \frac{d}{dx} ln(f(x)) &= \frac{d}{dx}[4ln(x) + \frac{1}{2}ln(x+2) - xln(3) - ln(x^2 + 1)] \\ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} &= \left[\frac{4}{x} + \frac{1}{2(x+2)} - ln(3) - \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1}\right] &&\text{Using (4)}\\ \end{align}$$

We are not finished yet because we need $f'(x)$ by itself, we can achieve this by multiply $f(x)$ to the other side

$$\begin{align} \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} &= \left[\frac{4}{x} + \frac{1}{2(x+2)} - ln(3) - \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1}\right] \\ f'(x) &= f(x)\left[\frac{4}{x} + \frac{1}{2(x+2)} - ln(3) - \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1}\right] \\ f'(x) &= \left[\frac{x^4\sqrt{x+2}}{3^x(x^2 + 1)}\right]\left[\frac{4}{x} + \frac{1}{2(x+2)} - ln(3) - \frac{2x}{x^2 + 1}\right] \\ \end{align}$$

And we are done! We could simplify if we wanted to, but it's fine to leave like this

To recap, here are the steps

  1. Take $ln$ of both sides
  2. Use log rules and simplify the right side
  3. Take derivative of both sides
  4. Multiply original function on both sides
Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dx}x^x$

    $$\begin{align} f(x) &= x^x \\ ln(f(x)) &= xln(x) \\ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} &= (x)(\frac{1}{x}) + (1)(ln(x)) &&\text{Using Product Rule} \\ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} &= 1 + ln(x) &&\text{Simplify} \\ f'(x) &= f(x)\left[1 + ln(x)\right] \\ f'(x) &= (x^x)\left[1 + ln(x)\right] \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} f(x) &= \frac{e^{4x}(x+3)^8}{(2x + 2)^2\sqrt{x}} \\ ln(f(x)) &= ln(\frac{e^{4x}(x+3)^8}{(2x + 2)^2\sqrt{x}}) \\ ln(f(x)) &= 4xln(e) + 8ln(x+3) - 2ln(2x + 2) - \frac{1}{2}ln(x) \\ ln(f(x)) &= 4x + 8ln(x+3) - 2ln(2x + 2) - \frac{1}{2}ln(x) &&\text{Simplify}\\ \frac{d}{dx}ln(f(x)) &= \frac{d}{dx} \left[4x + 8ln(x+3) - 2ln(2x + 2) - \frac{1}{2}ln(x)\right]\\ \frac{f'(x)}{f(x)} &= 4 + \frac{8}{x+3} - \frac{4}{2x + 2} - \frac{1}{2x} \\ f'(x) &= f(x)\left[4 + \frac{8}{x+3} - \frac{4}{2x + 2} - \frac{1}{2x}\right] \\ f'(x) &= \left[\frac{e^{4x}(x+3)^8}{(2x + 2)^2\sqrt{x}}\right]\left[4 + \frac{8}{x+3} - \frac{4}{2x + 2} - \frac{1}{2x}\right] \end{align}$$
