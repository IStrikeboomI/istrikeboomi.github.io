A derivative of a function $f(x)$ can be formally written as:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \tag{1}$$

But, what does that even mean?

The derivative of a function $f(x)$ gives a new function that can be written many ways, commonly $f'(x)$

At a point $x = c$ (also can be written as $(c,f(c))$), then $f'(c)$ gives the slope of the tangent line at $x = c$

The tangent line at a point is the line that "just touches" the curve of $f(x)$ at $x=c$

In the above graph the blue curve is a polynomial and the red line is the tangent line at a point

Why Is The Tangent Line Important?

The tangent line can be interpreted as the instantaneous rate of change

This is relevant because rate of change is used in many fields

For example if we had a function $d(t)$ which gave the distance in a time $t$

Then the derivative $d'(t)$ would be the rate of change of distance aka velocity

TLDR, the derivative is rate of change

So How Do We Calculate It?

We can use the slope formula with rise over run

$$ \frac{Rise}{Run} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \tag{2} $$

The problem with this is we need 2 points, but we only have 1 point for a tangent line

So what we do is to create a second point that is some distance on the x-axis away from the first point at $x=c$

Point 1: $(c,f(c))$

Point 2: $(c +\Delta x,f(c + \Delta x))$

In Point 2, $\Delta x$ is the distance from the the first point's x coordinate

We can now apply the slope formula above

$$\frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{f(c + \Delta x) - f(c)}{c +\Delta x - c} = \frac{f(c + \Delta x) - f(c)}{\Delta x}$$

This doesn't look like a tangent line, because it's not, it's a secant line because it crosses at two points

But, if we make the distance $\Delta X$ smaller, the points get closer to each other

when the points get closer, the secant line resembles a tangent line

So what we do for a derivative is to the use the slope formula (2) above and take $\Delta x$ closer to 0 with a limit

The reason we take $\Delta x$ to 0 is because we want the distance $\Delta x$ between the points to be as small as possible

Combining this all together we get the limit definition of a derivative:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \tag{1}$$

For derivative of $f'(x)$ at a point $x = c$:

$$f'(c) = \lim_{h \to c}\frac{f(h) - f(c)}{h - c} \tag{2}$$

We also change the $\Delta x$ to an $h$ because it's easier to write, but they represent the same thing

Whenever taking the derivative of the function there are many different ways to express the derivative of a fucntion

Two common are:

$$\frac{d}{dx}f(x)$$

$$f'(x)$$

Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dx} 4x$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}4x &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{4(x+h) - 4x}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{4x + 4h - 4x}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{4h}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}4 \\ &= 4 \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx} x^2 + 3x - 2$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} x^2 + 3x - 2 &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(x+h)^2 + 3(x+h) - 2 - (x^2 + 3x - 2)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) + 3x+3h - 2 - (x^2 + 3x - 2)}{h} &&\text{Distribute polynomials}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{h^2 + 2xh + 3h + x^2 + 3x - 2 - (x^2 + 3x - 2)}{h} &&\text{Rearrange Terms}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{h^2 + 2xh + 3h}{h} &&\text{Cancel Like Terms}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{h(h + 2x + 3)}{h} &&\text{Factor out $h$}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}(h + 2x + 3) &&\text{Cancel Like Terms}\\ &= 2x + 3 &&\text{Direct substitution}\\ \end{align}$$

  • Find slope of the tangent line at $x=2$ for $f(x) = 2x^2 + 1$

    We can solve this either using definition (1) and then plugging in x = 2

    Or, we can use definition (2) directly

    Using definition (1):

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}2x^2 + 1 &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{2(x+h)^2 + 1 - (2x^2 + 1)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{2(x^2 + 2xh + h^2) + 1 - (2x^2 + 1)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{2x^2 + 4xh + 2h^2 + 1 - (2x^2 + 1)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{4xh + 2h^2}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{2h(2x + h^2)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} 2(2x + h^2) \\ &= 2(2x) \\ &= 4x \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} f'(x) &= 4x \\ f(2) &= 8 \end{align}$$

    Using definition (2):

    $$\begin{align} f'(2) &= \lim_{h \to 2}\frac{2h^2 + 1 - f(2)}{h - 2} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 2}\frac{2h^2 + 1 - 9}{h - 2} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 2}\frac{2h^2 - 8}{h - 2} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 2}\frac{2(h^2 - 4)}{h - 2} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 2}\frac{2(h - 2)(h + 2)}{h - 2} &&\text{Using difference of two squares} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 2} 2(h + 2) \\ &= 2(2 + 2) &&\text{Using direct substitution}\\ &= 8\\ \end{align}$$

  • Find $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \sqrt{x} &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\sqrt{x + h} - \sqrt{x}}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\sqrt{x + h} - \sqrt{x}}{h} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}}{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}}\right) &&\text{Multiply by conjugate} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(\sqrt{x + h})^2 - (\sqrt{x})^2}{h(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x})} &&\text{Using Difference of Two Squares}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{x + h - x}{h(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x})} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{h}{h(\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x})} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{1}{\sqrt{x + h} + \sqrt{x}} \\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x + 0} + \sqrt{x}} &&\text{Using Direct Substitution}\\ &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt{x}} \\ &= \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} \\ f'(x) &= \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} \end{align}$$
