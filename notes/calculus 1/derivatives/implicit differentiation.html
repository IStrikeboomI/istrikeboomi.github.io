Imagine the implicit function $e^{xy} = 2y + x^2$

This function still has tangent lines at points, like at (-1, 0)

Since this function has tangent lines, we can find the derivative for this

The end goal that we want is the derivative, $y'$ or $\frac{dy}{dx}$

The strategy we will use is implicit differentation

Implicit differentation steps:

  1. Take derivative of both sides (when you are taking derivative of y, you treat it like a normal derivative and then Multiply by $y'$ or $\frac{dy}{dx}$)
  2. Distribute $\frac{dy}{dx}$
  3. Move all $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to one side and everything without $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to the other side
  4. Factor out $\frac{dy}{dx}$
  5. Isolate $\frac{dy}{dx}$ by dividing the factored out part to the other side

The reason we can treat y as a normal derivative and then multiply by $\frac{dy}{dx}$:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}y = \frac{dy}{dx} \end{align}$$

And then use chain rule to multiply by derivative of $y$ (which is $\frac{dy}{dx}$)

For example:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} y^3 \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^3 & g(x) = y \\ \hline f'(x) = 3x^2 & g'(x) = \frac{dy}{dx} \end{array} \\ \end{align}$$

For example:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} y^3 = 2y^3 \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} \end{align}$$

Lets solve the implicit function above:

$$\begin{align} e^{xy} &= 2y + x^2 \\ \frac{d}{dx} e^{xy} &= \frac{d}{dx} 2y + x^2 &&\text{Step 1}\\ e^{xy} \cdot (\frac{d}{dx}(xy) &= 2\frac{dy}{dx} + 2x &&\text{Still Step 1}\\ e^{xy} \cdot \left((1)(y) + (x)(\frac{dy}{dx})\right) &= 2\frac{dy}{dx} + 2x &&\text{Still Step 1}\\ e^{xy}y + e^{xy}x\frac{dy}{dx} &= 2\frac{dy}{dx} + 2x &&\text{Step 2}\\ e^{xy}x\frac{dy}{dx} - 2\frac{dy}{dx} &= 2x - e^{xy}y &&\text{Step 3}\\ \frac{dy}{dx}\left(e^{xy}x - 2\right) &= 2x - e^{xy}y &&\text{Step 4}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{2x - e^{xy}y}{e^{xy}x - 2} &&\text{Step 5}\\ \end{align}$$

And now we are done!

If you want the slope of the tangent line at a point $(x,y)$ you plug it into the final $\frac{dy}{dx}$

If you want the equation of the tangent line at a point $(x_1,y_1)$ you use use point-slope form

$$\begin{align} y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \end{align}$$

Where $x_1$ is your x-coordinate, $y_1$ is your y-coordinate, and $m$ is your $\frac{dy}{dx}$ evaluated at $(x_1,y_1)$

So the equation of the function above at $(1,0)$ is

$$\begin{align} y - 0 &= \frac{2(1) - e^{(1)(0)}(0)}{e^{(1)(0)}(1) - 2}(x - 1) \\ y &= \frac{2 - 0}{e(0) - 2}(x - 1) \\ y &= \frac{2}{1 - 2}(x - 1) \\ y &= -2(x - 1) \\ y &= -2x + 2 \\ \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dx} \left(x^{2}y+xy^{3}=3\right)$

    For this example I'm going to use $y'$ instead of $\frac{dy}{dx}$, but it means the same thing

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} x^{2}y+xy^{3} &= 3 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \left(x^{2}y+xy^{3}\right) &= \frac{d}{dx}(3) \\ (2x)(y)+(x^2)(y') + (1)(y^3) + (x)(3y^2y') &= 0 \\ 2xy + y'x^2 + y^3 + y'3xy^2 &= 0 \\ y'x^2+ y'3xy^2 &= -y^3 - 2xy \\ y'(x^2+ 3xy^2) &= -y^3 - 2xy \\ y' &= \frac{-y^3 - 2xy}{x^2+ 3xy^2} \\ \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dt} \left(cos(2t)+4sin(3y)=2y\right)$

    For this example I'm going to be taking the derivative in respect to $t$ instead of $x$ to show how to do it for other variables

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dt} cos(2t)+4sin(3y) &= 2y \\ \frac{d}{dt} \left(cos(2t)+4sin(3y)\right) &= \frac{d}{dt}\left(2y\right) \\ -2sin(2t) + 12cos(3y)\frac{dy}{dt} &= 2\frac{dy}{dt} \\ 12cos(3y)\frac{dy}{dt} - 2\frac{dy}{dt} &= 2sin(2t) \\ \frac{dy}{dt} \left(12cos(3y) - 2\right) &= 2sin(2t) \\ \frac{dy}{dt} &= \frac{2sin(2t)}{12cos(3y) - 2} \\ \frac{dy}{dt} &= \frac{sin(2t)}{6cos(3y) - 1} \\ \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dy} \left(\sqrt{yx} + sin(y) + e^x = x + y + 2\right)$

    In this example we are differentating in respect to y instead of x

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dy} \sqrt{yx} + sin(y) + e^x &= x + y + 2\\ \frac{d}{dy} \left(\sqrt{yx} + sin(y) + e^x\right) &= \frac{d}{dy}\left(x + y + 2\right)\\ \frac{(1)(x) + (\frac{dx}{dy})(y)}{2\sqrt{yx}} + cos(y) + \frac{dx}{dy}e^x &= \frac{dx}{dy} + 1 + 0\\ \frac{x+ y\frac{dx}{dy}}{2\sqrt{yx}} + cos(y) + \frac{dx}{dy}e^x &= \frac{dx}{dy} + 1\\ \frac{x}{2\sqrt{yx}} + \frac{y\frac{dx}{dy}}{2\sqrt{yx}} + cos(y) + \frac{dx}{dy}e^x &= \frac{dx}{dy} + 1\\ \frac{y\frac{dx}{dy}}{2\sqrt{yx}} + \frac{dx}{dy}e^x - \frac{dx}{dy} &= 1 - cos(y) - \frac{x}{2\sqrt{yx}} \\ \frac{dx}{dy}\left[\frac{y}{2\sqrt{yx}} + e^x - 1\right] &= 1 - cos(y) - \frac{x}{2\sqrt{yx}} \\ \frac{dx}{dy} &= \frac{1 - cos(y) - \frac{x}{2\sqrt{yx}}}{\frac{y}{2\sqrt{yx}} + e^x - 1} \\ \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx} \left(x^2 + y^2 = 3^2\right)$

    This is also the function for a circle with a radius of 3

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} x^2 + y^2 &= 3^2\\ \frac{d}{dx} \left(x^2 + y^2\right) &= \frac{d}{dx} \left(3^2\right)\\ 2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} &= 0\\ 2y\frac{dy}{dx} &= -2x\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{-2x}{2y}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{x}{y}\\ \end{align}$$
