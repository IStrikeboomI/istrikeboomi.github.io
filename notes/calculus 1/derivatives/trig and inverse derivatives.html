Here are the derivatives for all the trig functions

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} sin(x) &= cos(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx} cos(x) &= -sin(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx} tan(x) &= sec^2(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx} cot(x) &= -csc^2(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx} sec(x) &= sec(x)tan(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx} csc(x) &= -csc(x)cot(x) \\ \end{align}$$

And for the trig inverse functions

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} sin^{-1}(x) &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \\ \frac{d}{dx} cos^{-1}(x) &= -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \\ \frac{d}{dx} tan^{-1}(x) &= \frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \\ \frac{d}{dx} cot^{-1}(x) &= -\frac{1}{x^2 + 1} \\ \frac{d}{dx} sec^{-1}(x) &= \frac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \\ \frac{d}{dx} csc^{-1}(x) &= -\frac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} \\ \end{align}$$

We can get the derivatives of the trig function by only using $sin(x)$ and $cos(x)$

$$\begin{align} tan(x) &= \frac{sin(x)}{cos(x)} \\ cot(x) &= \frac{cos(x)}{sin(x)} \\ sec(x) &= \frac{1}{cos(x)} \\ csc(x) &= \frac{1}{sin(x)} \\ \end{align}$$

We can use quotient rule and the trig property $cos^2(x) + sin^2(x) = 1$ to solve them

For the inverse trig functions, we can either take inverse of both sides and use implicit differentation or the inverse function rule

Then we need to use variations of the sum of squared trig functions to solve them further

Proofs
  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}sin(x)$

    First establish two important limits

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{sin(x)}{x} &= 1 \tag{1}\\ \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{cos(x) - 1}{x} &= 0 \tag{2} \end{align}$$

    Derivative:

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} sin(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{sin(x+h) - sin(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(sin(x)cos(h) + cos(x)sin(h)) - sin(x)}{h} &&\text{Using $sin(\alpha + \beta) = sin(\alpha)cos(\beta) + cos(\alpha)sin(\beta)$} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{sin(x)cos(h) - sin(x) + cos(x)sin(h)}{h} &&\text{Rearrange terms} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{sin(x)cos(h) - sin(x)}{h} + \frac{ cos(x)sin(h)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{sin(x)(cos(h) - 1)}{h} + \frac{ cos(x)sin(h)}{h}\right) \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{sin(x)(cos(h) - 1)}{h}\right) + \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{ cos(x)sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Using limit sum property}\\ &= sin(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{cos(h) - 1}{h}\right) + cos(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Terms with an x can be factored out since they don't involve the limit variable of h}\\ &= sin(x)\left(0\right) + cos(x)\left(1\right) &&\text{By (1) and (2)}\\ &= cos(x) \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}cos(x)$

    First establish two important limits

    $$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{sin(x)}{x} &= 1 \tag{1}\\ \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{cos(x) - 1}{x} &= 0 \tag{2} \end{align}$$

    Derivative:

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} cos(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{cos(x+h) - cos(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(cos(x)cos(h) - sin(x)sin(h)) - cos(x)}{h} &&\text{Using $cos(\alpha + \beta) = cos(\alpha)cos(\beta) - sin(\alpha)sin(\beta)$} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{cos(x)cos(h) - cos(x) - sin(x)sin(h)}{h} &&\text{Rearrange terms} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{cos(x)cos(h) - cos(x)}{h} - \frac{ sin(x)sin(h)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{cos(x)(cos(h) - 1)}{h} - \frac{ sin(x)sin(h)}{h}\right) \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{cos(x)(cos(h) - 1)}{h}\right) - \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{sin(x)sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Using limit sum property}\\ &= cos(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{cos(h) - 1}{h}\right) - sin(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Terms with an x can be factored out since they don't involve the limit variable of h}\\ &= cos(x)\left(0\right) - sin(x)\left(1\right) &&\text{By (1) and (2)}\\ &= -sin(x) \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}tan(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}tan(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}\frac{sin(x)}{cos(x)}\\ &= \frac{(cos(x))(cos(x)) - (-sin(x))(sin(x))}{cos^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{cos^2(x) + sin^2(x)}{cos^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{1}{cos^2(x)} &&\text{Using $cos^2(x) + sin^2(x) = 1$}\\ &= sec^2(x)\\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}cot(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}cot(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}\frac{cos(x)}{sin(x)}\\ &= \frac{(-sin(x))(sin(x))-(cos(x))(cos(x))}{sin^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{-sin^2(x) - cos^2(x)}{sin^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{-(sin^2(x) + cos^2(x))}{sin^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{-1}{sin^2(x)} &&\text{Using $cos^2(x) + sin^2(x) = 1$}\\ &= -csc^2(x)\\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}sec(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}sec(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}\frac{1}{cos(x)}\\ &= \frac{(0)(cos(x))-(-sin(x))(1)}{cos^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{sin(x)}{cos^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{1}{cos(x)} \cdot \frac{sin(x)}{cos(x)}\\ &= sec(x)tan(x)\\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}csc(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}csc(x) &= \frac{d}{dx}\frac{1}{sin(x)}\\ &= \frac{(0)(sin(x))-(cos(x))(1)}{sin^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{-cos(x)}{sin^2(x)}\\ &= -\frac{1}{sin(x)} \cdot \frac{cos(x)}{sin(x)}\\ &= -csc(x)cot(x)\\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}sin^{-1}(x)$

    $$\begin{align} y &= sin^{-1}(x) \\ sin(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx} sin(y) &= \frac{d}{dx} x \\ cos(y)\frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{cos(y)} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-sin^2(y)}} &&\text{Using Rearranged version of $cos^2(x) + sin^2(x) = 1$}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}cos^{-1}(x)$

    $$\begin{align} y &= cos^{-1}(x) \\ cos(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx} cos(y) &= \frac{d}{dx} x \\ -sin(y)\frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{-sin(y)} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{-\sqrt{1-cos^2(y)}} &&\text{Using Rearranged version of $cos^2(x) + sin^2(x) = 1$}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}tan^{-1}(x)$

    $$\begin{align} y &= tan^{-1}(x) \\ tan(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx}tan(y) &= \frac{d}{dx}x \\ sec^2(y)\frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{sec^2(y)} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{1 + tan^2(x)} &&\text{Using $tan^2(x) + 1 = sec^2(x)$}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{1 + x^2} \\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}cot^{-1}(x)$

    $$\begin{align} y &= cot^{-1}(x) \\ cot(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx}cot(y) &= \frac{d}{dx}x \\ -csc^2(y)\frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{-csc^2(y)} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{-(1 + cot^2(x))} &&\text{Using $1 + cot^2(x) = csc^2(x)$}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{1}{1 + x^2} \\ \end{align}$$

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}sec^{-1}(x)$

    $$\begin{align} y &= sec^{-1}(x) \\ sec(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx}sec(y) &= \frac{d}{dx}x \\ sec(y)tan(y)\frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{sec(y)tan(y)} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{sec(y)\sqrt{sec^2(y) - 1}} &&\text{Using Rearranged version of $tan^2(x) + 1 = sec^2(x)$}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} &&\text{$|x|$ because $sec(y)tan(y)$ has to be positive (Read below for more)}\\ \end{align}$$

    The reason there is an absolute value sign is because if $y=sec^{-1}(x)$, the range, or the angle is $[0,\pi/2) \cup (\pi/2,\pi]$

    This places the angle in the first and second quadrant

    The $sec(y)tan(y)$ can be rewritten as $\frac{sin(y)}{cos^2(y)}$

    In the first and second quadrant, $sin(\theta)$ is always positive, and the $cos^2(y)$ in the denominator is being squared, so it always postive

    This means the $sec(y)tan(y)$ has to be positive

    The $\sqrt{x^2 - 1}$ is always positive, which means we need to make sure the $x$ is always positive, so we absolute value it

  • Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}csc^{-1}(x)$

    $$\begin{align} y &= csc^{-1}(x) \\ csc(y) &= x \\ \frac{d}{dx}csc(y) &= \frac{d}{dx}x \\ -csc(y)cot(y)\frac{dy}{dx} &= 1 \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{1}{-csc(y)cot(y)} \\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{1}{csc(y)\sqrt{csc^2(y) - 1}} &&\text{Using Rearranged version of $1 + cot^2(x) = csc^2(x)$}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2 - 1}} &&\text{$|x|$ because $csc(y)cot(y)$ has to be positive (Read below for more)}\\ \end{align}$$

    The reason there is an absolute value sign is because if $y=csc^{-1}(x)$, the range, or the angle is $[-\pi/2,0) \cup (0,\pi/2]$

    This places the angle in the first and fourth quadrant

    The $csc(y)cot(y)$ can be rewritten as $\frac{cos(y)}{sin^2(y)}$

    In the first and fourth quadrant, $cos(\theta)$ is always positive, and the $sin^2(y)$ in the denominator is being squared, so it always postive

    This means the $csc(y)cot(y)$ has to be positive

    The $\sqrt{x^2 - 1}$ is always positive, which means we need to make sure the $x$ is always positive, so we absolute value it
