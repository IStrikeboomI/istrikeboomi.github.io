The derivative of $ln(x)$ is

$$\frac{d}{dx} ln(x) = \frac{1}{x}$$

For logarithims of other bases such as $log_2(x), log_{10}(x), log_5(x)$

You can utilize the change of base formula for logs

$$ log_a(x) = \frac{ln(x)}{ln(a)} $$

Since $ln(a)$ is a constant, you can factor it out

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}log_a(x) &= \frac{1}{ln(a)} \left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \\ &= \frac{1}{ln(a)x} \end{align}$$

Proof 1

Definition of $e \approx 2.71828$:

$$\begin{align} e &= \lim_{n \to \infty}{{(1+\frac{1}{n})}^n} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}{{(1+h)}^{1/h}} &&\text{let $h = \frac{1}{n}$, as $n \rightarrow \infty, h \rightarrow 0$ } \label{e def} \tag{1} \end{align}$$

Derivative:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}ln(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{ln(x + h) - ln(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{ln(\frac{x + h}{x})}{h} &&\text{Using log property } log(a/b) = log(a) - log(b) \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{h}\right) ln(\frac{x + h}{x}) &&\text{Using log property } log(a^b) = b\cdot log(a)\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} ln((\frac{x + h}{x})^{1/h})\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} ln((1 + \frac{h}{x})^{1/h})\\ &= \lim_{t \to 0} ln((1 + t)^{1/tx}) &&\text{Let $t = \frac{h}{x} \rightarrow \frac{1}{h} = \frac{1}{tx}$, as $h \rightarrow 0, t \rightarrow 0$}\\ &= \lim_{t \to 0} ln((1 + t)^{\frac{1}{t} \cdot \frac{1}{x}})\\ &= \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{1}{x}ln((1 + t)^{\frac{1}{t}}) &&\text{Using log property } log(a^b) = b\cdot log(a)\\ &= \frac{1}{x} \cdot \lim_{t \to 0} ln((1 + t)^{\frac{1}{t}}) &&\text{The $\frac{1}{x}$ is not affected by the limit (it can be factored out)}\\ &= \frac{1}{x} \cdot ln(\lim_{t \to 0}(1 + t)^{\frac{1}{t}}) &&\text{In a limit of a continous function, they can both be flipped}\\ &= \frac{1}{x} \cdot ln(e) &&\text{By (1)}\\ &= \frac{1}{x} \end{align}$$

Proof 2

Using the inverse function rule

$$ (f^{-1})'(x) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(x))} $$

Here,

$$ \begin{align} f(x) &= e^x \\ f'(x) &= e^x \\ f^{-1}(x) &= ln(x) \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} ln(x) &= \frac{1}{e^{ln(x)}} \\ &= \frac{1}{x} \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dx} ln(3x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}ln(3x) = \frac{3}{3x} &&\text{Using Chain Rule} \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}xln(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}xe^{x} &= (1)(ln(x)) + (x)(\frac{1}{x}) &&\text{Using Product Rule} \\ &= ln(x) + 1 \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx}ln(cos(x))$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}ln(cos(x)) &= \frac{-sin(x)}{cos(x)} &&\text{Using Chain Rule} \\ &= -tan(x) &&\text{Using } tan(x) = \frac{sin(x)}{cos(x)} \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx} log_{10}(2x^2 + 4x - 5)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} log_{10}(2x^2 + 4x - 5) &= \frac{4x + 4}{ln(10)(2x^2 + 4x - 5)} &&\text{Using Chain Rule} \\ \end{align}$$
