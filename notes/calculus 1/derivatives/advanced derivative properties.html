To solve more advanced derivatves, we need more advanced proeprties

Here we will talk about the product rule, quotient rule, and the chain rule

For the following, assume $f$ and $g$ are functions and they are differentiable (means $f'$ and $g'$ exists)

Product Rule:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \left(f(x) \cdot g(x)\right) &= \left(f'(x)\right) \cdot \left(g(x)\right) + \left(g'(x)\right) \cdot \left(f(x)\right) \\ &= \left(f'\right) \left(g\right) + \left(g'\right) \left(f\right) \\ &= f'(x)g(x) + g'(x)f(x) \end{align}$$

The product rule is used when you are multiplying two functions

For example:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \left(x^2 + 2x - 3\right)\left(x^3 - x + 2\right) \end{align}$$

To solve these advanced derivative properties, I like to use a table to denote $f(x), f'(x), g(x), g'(x)$

$$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^2 + 2x - 3 & g(x) = x^3 - x + 2 \\ \hline f'(x) = 2x + 2 & g'(x) = 3x^2 - 1 \end{array} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx} \left(x^2 + 2x - 3\right)\left(x^3 - x + 2\right) &= \left(2x + 2\right)\left(x^3 - x + 2\right) + \left(3x^2 - 1 \right)\left(x^2 + 2x - 3 \right) \end{align}$$

We could simplify this further, but it's fine if we leave it like this

Quotient Rule:

$$\begin{align} g(x) &\neq 0 \\ \frac{d}{dx} \left(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right) &= \frac{f'(x)g(x) - g'(x)f(x)}{g(x)^2} \end{align}$$

The quotient rule is used when dividing two functions

It's important that $g(x)$ is NOT 0, because you can't divide by 0

For Example:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \left(\frac{x^2 + 1}{\sin(x)}\right) \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^2 + 1 & g(x) = \sin(x) \\ \hline f'(x) = 2x & g'(x) = \cos(x) \end{array} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx} \left(\frac{x^2 + 1}{\sin(x)}\right) &= \frac{(2x)(\sin(x)) - (\cos(x))(x^2 + 1)}{\sin^2(x)}\\ &= \frac{2x\sin(x) - x^2\cos(x) - \cos(x)}{\sin^2(x)} \end{align}$$

Chain rule:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \end{align}$$

Or in Leibniz notation (where $u=g(x)$)

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} f(g(x)) = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du}\frac{du}{dx} \end{align}$$

Chain rule is used in composition of 2 functions

For example:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \cos(x^4 - x^2 + 4x - 1) \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \cos(x) & g(x) = x^4 - x^2 + 4x - 1 \\ \hline f'(x) = -\sin(x) & g'(x) = 4x^3 - 2x + 4 \end{array} \\ \\ \frac{d}{dx} \cos(x^4 - x^2 + 4x - 1) &= (4x^3 - 2x + 4) (-\sin(x^4 - x^2 + 4x - 1))\\ \end{align}$$

U\sing all the rules so far and the derivates of function, you can solve pretty much any derivatives

Whenever solving derivatives that need multiple properties, start from the outside functions and go inner

Practice Problems
  • $\frac{d}{dx} (x^2 + 8)^{14}$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} (x^2 + 8)^{14} \end{align}$$

    We could expand the expression and get a giant polynomial

    Or we could use chain rule

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^{14} & g(x) = x^2 + 8 \\ \hline f'(x) = 14x^{13} & g'(x) = 2x \end{array} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} (x^2 + 8)^{14} &= 14(x^2 + 8)^{13} \cdot (2x)\\ &= 28x(x^2 + 8)^{13} \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx} x\cos(2x^2)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} x\cos(2x^2) \end{align}$$

    We need to apply product rule

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x & g(x) = \cos(2x^2) \\ \hline f'(x) = 1 & g'(x) = ? \end{array} \end{align}$$

    To find the derivative of $g(x)$ we need to use chain rule

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \cos(x) & g(x) = 2x^2 \\ \hline f'(x) = -\sin(x) & g'(x) = 4x \end{array} \end{align}$$

    Putting this back into original table

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x & g(x) = \cos(2x^2) \\ \hline f'(x) = 1 & g'(x) = -4x\sin(2x^2) \end{array} \end{align}$$

    Now we can apply product rule

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} x\cos(2x^2) &= (1)(\cos(2x^2)) + (-4x\sin(2x^2))(x) \\ &= \cos(2x^2) - 4x^2\sin(2x^2) \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + x}}{\sin(\frac{1}{x})}$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + x}}{\sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)} \end{align}$$

    To solve first do quotient rule on outside

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + x} & g(x) = \sin(x^{-1}) \\ \hline f'(x) = ? & g'(x) = ? \end{array} \end{align}$$

    We need to use chain rule on both $f(x)$ and $g(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \sqrt{x^2 + x} = \frac{2x + 1}{2\sqrt{x^2 + x}} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sqrt{x} & g(x) = x^2 + x \\ \hline f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} & g'(x) = 2x + 1 \end{array} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \sin(x^{-1}) = -x^{-2}\cos(x^{-1}) \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sin(x) & g(x) = x^{-1} \\ \hline f'(x) = \cos(x) & g'(x) = -x^{-2} \end{array} \end{align}$$

    Putting this back into the table

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sqrt{x^2 + x} & g(x) = \sin(x^{-1}) \\ \hline f'(x) = \frac{2x + 1}{2\sqrt{x^2 + x}} & g'(x) = -x^{-2}\cos(x^{-1}) \end{array} \end{align}$$

    Putting this all together with the quotient rule

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + x}}{\sin\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)} &= \frac{(\frac{2x + 1}{2\sqrt{x^2 + x}})(\sin(x^{-1})) - (-x^{-2}\cos(x^{-1}))(\sqrt{x^2 + x}))}{\sin^2\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)} \\ &=\frac{\frac{2x + 1(\sin(x^{-1}))}{2\sqrt{x^2 + x}} + x^{-2}\cos(x^{-1})\sqrt{x^2 + x}}{\sin^2\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)} \end{align}$$

  • $\frac{d}{dx} \sin\left(\frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2}\right)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \sin\left(\frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2}\right) \end{align}$$

    We need to use chain rule and then quotient rule

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sin(x) & g(x) = \frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2} \\ \hline f'(x) = \cos(x) & g'(x) = ? \end{array} \end{align}$$

    We need to use quotient rule to find the derivative

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2} = \frac{(2x + 4)( x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2) - (3x^2 + 6x - 5)(x^2 + 4x - 1)}{(x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2)^2} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^2 + 4x - 1 & g(x) = x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2 \\ \hline f'(x) = 2x + 4 & g'(x) = 3x^2 + 6x - 5 \end{array} \end{align}$$

    We can plug this back into the original table

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = \sin(x) & g(x) = \frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2} \\ \hline f'(x) = \cos(x) & g'(x) = \frac{(2x + 4)( x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2) - (3x^2 + 6x - 5)(x^2 + 4x - 1)}{(x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2)^2} \end{array} \end{align}$$

    We can apply the chain rule now

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \sin\left(\frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2}\right) = \left[\frac{(2x + 4)( x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2) - (3x^2 + 6x - 5)(x^2 + 4x - 1)}{(x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2)^2}\right]\cos\left(\frac{x^2 + 4x - 1}{x^3 + 3x^2 - 5x - 2}\right) \end{align}$$

Proofs
  • Product Rule

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} f(x)g(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) + 0 - f(x)g(x)}{h} &&\text{Add 0}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) + (f(x+h)g(x) - f(x+h)g(x)) - f(x)g(x)}{h} &&\text{0 can be rewritten}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x+h)g(x) + f(x+h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)}{h} &&\text{Rearrange terms}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x+h)g(x)}{h} + \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)}{h} &&\text{U\sing limit sum property}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x+h)(g(x+h) - g(x))}{h} + \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(x)(f(x+h) - f(x))}{h} &&\text{Factor out terms}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}f(x+h)\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} + \lim_{h \to 0}g(x)\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} &&\text{U\sing limit product property}\\ &= f(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} + g(x)\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} &&\text{Solve outer limits}\\ &= f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) &&\text{Replace limits u\sing defintion of derivative}\\ &= f'(x)g(x) + g'(x)f(x) &&\text{Rearrange Terms}\\ \end{align}$$

  • Quotient Rule (Using limit defintion)

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{\frac{f(x + h)}{g(x + h)} - \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}}{h}\right)\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{\frac{f(x + h)g(x)}{g(x + h)g(x)} - \frac{f(x)g(x + h)}{g(x + h)g(x)}}{h}\right) &&\text{Make denominators match}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{\frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{g(x + h)g(x)}}{h}\right) &&\text{Add fractions}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{h} \cdot \frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) &&\text{Rewrite the divided by $h$}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{h \cdot g(x + h)g(x)}\right) &&\text{Multiply fractions}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)} \cdot \frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Split fractions}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{h}\right) &&\text{U\sing limit product property}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{f(x + h)g(x) + 0 - f(x)g(x + h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Add 0}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x) + f(x)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{h}\right) &&\text{0 can be rewritten}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) \cdot \left[\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x + h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)}{h} + \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x)g(x) - f(x)g(x + h)}{h}\right] &&\text{U\sing limit sum property}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) \cdot \left[\lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(x)(f(x + h)- f(x))}{h} + \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x)(g(x) - g(x + h))}{h}\right] &&\text{Factor out terms}\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{g(x + h)g(x)}\right) \cdot \left[\lim_{h \to 0}g(x) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x + h)- f(x)}{h} + \lim_{h \to 0}-f(x) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(x + h) - g(x)}{h}\right] &&\text{U\sing limit product property}\\ &= \left(\frac{1}{g(x + 0)g(x)}\right) \cdot \left[g(x) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(x + h)- f(x)}{h} - f(x) \cdot \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{g(x) - g(x + h)}{h}\right] &&\text{Solve some limits}\\ &= \left(\frac{1}{g(x)^2}\right) \cdot \left[g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)\right] &&\text{Replace some limits with derivative defintion}\\ &= \frac{g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)}{g(x)^2}&&\text{Multiply limits}\\ &= \frac{f'(x)g(x) - g'(x)f(x)}{g(x)^2}&&\text{Rearrange Terms}\\ \end{align}$$

  • Quotient Rule (Using product rule and chain rule)

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \frac{d}{dx} f(x) \cdot \frac{1}{g(x)} \\ &= \frac{d}{dx} f(x) \cdot g(x)^{-1} \end{align}$$

    U\sing product rule

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = f(x) & g(x) = g(x)^{-1} \\ \hline f'(x) = f'(x) & g'(x) = ? \end{array} \end{align}$$

    We need to use chain rule to find derivative of $g(x)$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}g(x)^{-1} = -\frac{g'(x)}{g(x)^2} \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = x^{-1} & g(x) = g(x) \\ \hline f'(x) = -x^{-2} & g'(x) = g'(x) \end{array} \end{align}$$

    Plugging this back into the original table

    $$\begin{align} &\begin{array} {c | c} f(x) = f(x) & g(x) = g(x)^{-1} \\ \hline f'(x) = f'(x) & g'(x) = -\frac{g'(x)}{g(x)^2} \end{array} \end{align}$$

    Now we can apply product rule

    $$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} f(x) \cdot g(x)^{-1} &= \frac{f'(x)}{g(x)} - \frac{g'(x)f(x)}{g(x)^2} \\ &= \frac{f'(x)g(x)}{g(x)^2} - \frac{g'(x)f(x)}{g(x)^2} &&\text{Match denominators of fractions so we can add both}\\ &= \frac{f'(x)g(x) - g'(x)f(x)}{g(x)^2} &&\text{Add fractions}\\ \end{align}$$

  • Chain rule

    $$\begin{align} (f \circ g)'(a) &= \lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(g(x) - f(g(a)))}{x-a} \\ &= \lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(g(x) - f(g(a)))}{x-a} \cdot \frac{g(x) - g(a)}{g(x)-g(a)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(g(x) - f(g(a)))}{g(x)-g(a)} \cdot \frac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a} &&\text{\since we are multiplying fractions, we can switch terms} \end{align}$$

    The function on the right looks the limit defintion of derivative at a point a

    The left function looks like the limit defintion of derivatve at point g(a)

    But this wont work if $g(x) = g(a)$ because $g(x) - g(a) = 0$ which leads to a divsion by 0

    We can fix this though by taking both cases

    Let's create a new function $k(x)$

    $$\begin{align} k(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{f(x) - f(g(a))}{x-g(a)} & \text{if } x \neq g(a)\\ f'(g(a)) & \text{if } x = g(a) \end{cases} \end{align}$$

    $k(x)$ is continous at $g(a)$ because:

    $$\begin{align} k(g(a)) = f'(g(a)) = \lim_{t \to g(a)} \frac{f(t) - f(g(a))}{t-g(a)} = \lim_{t \to g(a)} k(t) \end{align}$$

    So now we can do ($x \neq a$):

    $$\begin{align} (f \circ g)'(a) &= \lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(g(x) - f(g(a)))}{g(x)-g(a)} \cdot \frac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a} \\ &= \lim_{x \to a}k(g(x)) \cdot \lim_{x \to a}\frac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a} &&\text{Because left limit is $h(g(x))$} \\ &= k(g(a)) \cdot \lim_{x \to a}\frac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a} &&\text{Because $h(g(x))$ is continous} \\ &= k(g(a)) \cdot g'(a) &&\text{Replace limit with derivative} \\ &= f'(g(a)) \cdot g'(a) \end{align}$$
