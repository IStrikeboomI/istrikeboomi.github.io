This is a practical use of implicit differentiation

Related Rates is trying to find the rate of change of something at an instant, based on another rate of change

Remember, derivatives can be thought of as rate of change

The way we can relate them together is by using a formula that they share in common

Then we use Implicit Differentiation to get the rate of changes

We can then plug in known information to get the final rate of change

The best way to do Related Rates is to do many example, so there are no practice problems here

Example 1: Expanding Sphere

What is the rate of change of volume of a sphere that is expanding it's radius by .5 cm/s, when the radius of the sphere is 2 cm

Here the we are trying to find the rate of change of volume (by time), we can write this as $\frac{dV}{dt}$

We are given the rate of change of radius (by time), we can write this as $\frac{dr}{dt} = .5$

Since we are given it's a sphere, we can use the sphere volume formula

$$\begin{align} V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \end{align}$$

To find $\frac{dV}{dt}$, we differentiate both sides (in respect to $t$)

$$\begin{align} V = \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \\ \frac{d}{dt} V = \frac{d}{dt}\left[\frac{4}{3} \pi r^3\right] \\ \frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{4}{3} \pi (3r^2)\frac{dr}{dt} &&\text{Using Implicit Differentiation}\\ \frac{dV}{dt} = 4 \pi r^2\frac{dr}{dt}\\ \end{align}$$

So now we can get a formula for rate of change of volume, we just need to plug in the information

$$\begin{align} \frac{dV}{dt} = 4 \pi r^2\frac{dr}{dt}\\ \frac{dV}{dt} = 4 \pi (2)^2 (.5)\\ \frac{dV}{dt} = 8 \pi \approx 25.132741 \text{ cm/s}\\ \end{align}$$

Example 2: Falling Ladder

A 10 foot ladder is sliding down a wall at 4 in/s. How fast is the base of the ladder moving away from the wall when it is 6 feet from the wall?

A ladder leaning against a wall makes a right triangle, since the length of the ladder is 10 ft, it's the hypotenuse

To get the other side lengths, we will denote $y$ as the vertical side, and $x$ as the horizontal side, we can use the pythagorean theorem

Let's write down what we already know

$$\begin{align} x^2 + y^2 &= 10^2 \\ \frac{dy}{dt} &= -\frac{1}{3} &&\text{$-\frac{1}{3}$ because it's sliding down and conversion from in to ft} \\ x &= 6 \\ y &= \sqrt{10^2 - 6^2} = \sqrt{36} = 6 \end{align}$$

We are trying to find $\frac{dx}{dt}$, so let's differntiate both sides

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dt} x^2 + y^2 &= \frac{d}{dt}10^2 \\ 2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} &= 0 \\ 2x\frac{dx}{dt} &= - 2y\frac{dy}{dt} \\ \frac{dx}{dt} &= \frac{-y\frac{dy}{dt}}{x} \\ \frac{dx}{dt} &= \frac{-(6)(-\frac{1}{3})}{(6)} \\ \frac{dx}{dt} &= \frac{1}{3} \text{ ft/s} \\ &= 4 \text{ in/s} \\ \end{align}$$

Example 3: Changing Resistance

The formula for electrical resistance (in Ohms or $\Omega$) of two resistors in parallel is:

$$\begin{align} \frac{1}{R} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} \end{align}$$

Imagine you have two resistors in parallel, resistor one is increasing at .5 $\Omega$/sec, and resistor two is decreasing at 1 $\Omega$/sec

What is the rate of change of the total resistance when resistor one is at 100 $\Omega$ and resistor two is at 90 $\Omega$?

We are trying to find $\frac{dR}{dt}$

Let's find the total resistance at the given values first

$$\begin{align} \frac{1}{R} &= \frac{1}{100} + \frac{1}{90} \\ \frac{1}{R} &= \frac{19}{900}\\ R &= \frac{900}{19} = .02 \overline{11} \Omega\\ \end{align}$$

Let's write the values that we know

$$\begin{align} R_1 &= 100 \\ R_2 &= 90 \\ R &= \frac{900}{19} = .02 \overline{11} \Omega\\ \frac{dR_1}{dt} &= .5 \\ \frac{dR_2}{dt} &= -1 \\ \end{align}$$

So we will be finding rate of change when total resistance is at $.02 \overline{11} \Omega$

Let's now find a formula for rate of change, and plug in our known values

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dt}\frac{1}{R} &= \frac{d}{dt}\ \left[\frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2}\right] \\ -\frac{1}{R^2} \frac{dR}{dt}&= -\frac{1}{(R_1)^2}\frac{dR_1}{dt} -\frac{1}{(R_2)^2}\frac{dR_2}{dt} \\ \frac{dR}{dt}&= -R^2\left[-\frac{1}{(R_1)^2}\frac{dR_1}{dt} -\frac{1}{(R_2)^2}\frac{dR_2}{dt}\right] \\ \frac{dR}{dt}&= -\left(\frac{900}{19}\right)^2\left[-\frac{1}{100^2}(.5) -\frac{1}{90^2}(-1)\right] \\ &= -\left(\frac{810000}{361}\right)\left[-\frac{1}{20000} + \frac{1}{8100}\right] \\ &= -\frac{119}{722} \approx -0.164819 \Omega \end{align}$$

Example 4: Cone-Shaped Water Tank

Water is filling an upside-down cone shaped water tank at 10 ft/s. The radius of the tank is 20 ft and a height of 100 ft

What is the rate of change of the depth of water when the depth is 8 ft?

Since we are filling an upside-down cone, that means the water that is inside the cone is making another smaller cone

We can use related triangles to find the radius of water already in the tank

$$\begin{align} \frac{r}{h} &= \frac{R}{H} \\ \frac{r}{8} &= \frac{20}{100} \\ \frac{r}{8} &= \frac{1}{5} \\ r &= \frac{8}{5} \\ \end{align}$$

Let's write the values that we know

$$\begin{align} R &= 20 \\ H &= 100 \\ r &= \frac{8}{5} \\ h &= 8 \\ \frac{dV}{dt} &= 10 \\ \end{align}$$

We are trying to find $\frac{dh}{dt}$

We can use the volume of a cone formula, but plug in our $r$

$$\begin{align} V &= \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h \\ V &= \frac{1}{3} \pi \left(\frac{8}{5}\right)^2 h \\ \frac{d}{dt} V &= \frac{d}{dt} \left[\frac{1}{3} \pi \left(\frac{8}{5}\right)^2 h\right] \\ \frac{dV}{dt}&= \frac{1}{3} \pi \left(\frac{8}{5}\right)^2 \frac{dh}{dt}\\ \frac{dV}{dt}&= \frac{64\pi}{75}\frac{dh}{dt}\\ \frac{dh}{dt}&= \frac{75}{64 \pi}\frac{dV}{dt} \\ \frac{dh}{dt} &= \frac{75}{64 \pi}(10) \\ \frac{dh}{dt} &= \frac{365}{32 \pi} = 11.71785 \text{ ft/s} \\ \end{align}$$
