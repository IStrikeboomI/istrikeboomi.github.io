The derivatives of $\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x)$ are:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx}\sin(x) &= \cos(x) \\ \frac{d}{dx}\cos(x) &= -\sin(x) \end{align}$$

One intresting thing about this is if you take repeated derivatives of sine or cosine, you get into a chain

$$\begin{align} f(x) &= \sin(x) \\ f'(x) &= \cos(x) \\ f''(x) &= -\sin(x) \\ f'''(x) &= -\cos(x) \\ f^{(4)}(x) &= \sin(x) &&\text{Notice we are back to the original function}\\ f^{(5)}(x) &= \sin(x) \\ \end{align}$$

Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x)$

First establish two important limits

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin(x)}{x} &= 1 \tag{1}\\ \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\cos(x) - 1}{x} &= 0 \tag{2} \end{align}$$

Derivative:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \sin(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\sin(x+h) - \sin(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(\sin(x)\cos(h) + \cos(x)\sin(h)) - \sin(x)}{h} &&\text{Using $\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin(\alpha)\cos(\beta) + \cos(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\sin(x)\cos(h) - \sin(x) + \cos(x)\sin(h)}{h} &&\text{Rearrange terms} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\sin(x)\cos(h) - \sin(x)}{h} + \frac{ \cos(x)\sin(h)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\sin(x)(\cos(h) - 1)}{h} + \frac{ \cos(x)\sin(h)}{h}\right) \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\sin(x)(\cos(h) - 1)}{h}\right) + \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{ \cos(x)\sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Using limit sum property}\\ &= \sin(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\cos(h) - 1}{h}\right) + \cos(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Terms with an x can be factored out since they don't involve the limit variable of h}\\ &= \sin(x)\left(0\right) + \cos(x)\left(1\right) &&\text{By (1) and (2)}\\ &= \cos(x) \end{align}$$

Proof of $\frac{d}{dx}\cos(x)$

First establish two important limits

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\sin(x)}{x} &= 1 \tag{1}\\ \lim_{x \to 0}\frac{\cos(x) - 1}{x} &= 0 \tag{2} \end{align}$$

Derivative:

$$\begin{align} \frac{d}{dx} \cos(x) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\cos(x+h) - \cos(x)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{(\cos(x)\cos(h) - \sin(x)\sin(h)) - \cos(x)}{h} &&\text{Using $\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos(\alpha)\cos(\beta) - \sin(\alpha)\sin(\beta)$} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\cos(x)\cos(h) - \cos(x) - \sin(x)\sin(h)}{h} &&\text{Rearrange terms} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{\cos(x)\cos(h) - \cos(x)}{h} - \frac{ \sin(x)\sin(h)}{h} \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\cos(x)(\cos(h) - 1)}{h} - \frac{ \sin(x)\sin(h)}{h}\right) \\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\cos(x)(\cos(h) - 1)}{h}\right) - \lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\sin(x)\sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Using limit sum property}\\ &= \cos(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\cos(h) - 1}{h}\right) - \sin(x)\lim_{h \to 0}\left(\frac{\sin(h)}{h}\right) &&\text{Terms with an x can be factored out since they don't involve the limit variable of h}\\ &= \cos(x)\left(0\right) - \sin(x)\left(1\right) &&\text{By (1) and (2)}\\ &= -\sin(x) \end{align}$$
