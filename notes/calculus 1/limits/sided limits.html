When solving some limits, they might approach different values from either side

For example, take a look at the following graph:

$$\begin{align} f(-1) &= 0 \\ \lim_{x \to -1} f(x) &= DNE \end{align}$$

When approaching $-1$ from the left, you get 1

When approaching $-1$ from the right, you get -1

The way we solve this is to use sided limits

The way you write sided limits are the following

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) &&\text{Approaching $c$ from the Right} \\ \lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) &&\text{Approaching $c$ from the Left} \\ \end{align}$$

Notice the $+$ and the $-$ in the exponent position of the value the limit is approaching

The way I remember which is if there is a $+$, we start with a bigger number and approach $c$

The way I remember which is if there is a $-$, we start with a smaller number and approach $c$

Let's apply this to the function above

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to -1^+} f(x) &= -1 \\ \lim_{x \to -1^-} f(x) &= 1 \\ \end{align}$$

In order for a regular limit (not sided) to exist, both sides of the limit must be the same value

$$\begin{align} \lim_{x \to c} f(x) \text{ exists if and only if }\\ \lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) = \lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) \end{align}$$

Sided limits have the same properties as regular limits

It's also important to note a sided limit can't approach infinity

This is because there is nothing larger to the other side of infinity

    There is an asymptopte on the right side going to $-\infty$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_\limits{x \to 0^+} ln(x) = -\infty \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \lim_\limits{x \to -2^+} f(x) \\ \lim_\limits{x \to -2^-} f(x) \\ f(3) \\ \lim_\limits{x \to 3^+} f(x) \\ \lim_\limits{x \to 3^-} f(x) \\ \end{align}$$

  • Show $\lim_\limits{x \to 3} \sqrt{x-3}$ is DNE

    We show the limit is DNE by find both sides of the limit

    $$\begin{align} \lim_\limits{x \to 3^-} \sqrt{x-3} = DNE \\ \lim_\limits{x \to 3^+} \sqrt{x-3} = 0 \\ \end{align}$$

    Since both sided limits are not the same, the limit does not exist

  • Find $\lim_\limits{x \to 4} f(x)$ of the piecewise function

    $$\begin{align} \lim_\limits{x \to 4} f(x) \end{align}$$

    Where $f(x)$ is

    $$\begin{align} f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 - 4x & \text{if } x < 4\\ sin(\pi x) & \text{if } x > 4 \end{cases} \end{align}$$

    The way we would find the limit at 4, is to take the sided limit on both sides

    When we approach $f(x)$ from the left, $x$ is smaller than $4$, so it's the first case

    When we approach $f(x)$ from the right, $x$ is larger than $4$, so it's the second case

    $$\begin{align} \lim_\limits{x \to 4^+} f(x) = \lim_\limits{x \to 4^+} x^2 - 4x &= (4)^2 - 4(4) = 16 - 16 = 0 \\ \lim_\limits{x \to 4^-} f(x) = \lim_\limits{x \to 4^-} sin(\pi x) &= sin(4\pi) = 0 \end{align}$$

    Since both sided limits are the same, the limit does exist

    $$\begin{align} \lim_\limits{x \to 4} f(x) = 0 \end{align}$$
