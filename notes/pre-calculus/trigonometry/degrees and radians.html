So far when measuring angles (in $\theta$, pronounced thay-tuh) we only know degrees (marked with an $^\circ$), like $0^\circ, 45^\circ, 23^\circ$, etc.

When we deal with trigonometry, we typically use degrees and another way to do angles, radians (marked with rad)

Similar to how degrees goes from $0^\circ$ to $360^\circ$, radians go from $0$ to $2\pi$

As you can see in the circles above, degrees and radians correlate and represent the same fundamental idea, angles

Another way to intrepret radian is 1 radian (approx $57.2958^\circ$) is the angle where the arc length of the circle is equal to the radius

In the circle above, $\theta = 1 \text{ rad}$

This is also why radians are in multiples of $\pi$

If you had a circle of radius $r$, then the circumference would be $2r\pi$

For a circle of radius $1$, the full arc length would be the whole circle so it's full arc length is $2\pi$, so the radians of a full circle is $2\pi$

Conversions

To convert from degrees to radians:

$$\begin{align} \text{degrees} \cdot \frac{\pi}{180^\circ} = \text{radians} \end{align}$$

To convert from radians to degrees:

$$\begin{align} \text{radians} \cdot \frac{180^\circ}{\pi} = \text{degrees} \end{align}$$

The way these formulas are derived are just from the equation of 2 ratios

$$\begin{align} \frac{\text{radians}}{\pi} = \frac{\text{degrees}}{180^\circ} \end{align}$$

Why Are Radians Important?

A lot of trigonometric functions take in both degrees and radians, but radians is usaully default

The reason why we use radians is because it makes the math easier

Splitting the circle into 360 parts for degrees is just an arbitrary amount people set it to

Radians are naturally a part of the circle (from the radius of the circle) so it can be expressed in terms of a circle

Also as you will see later on in calculus, it makes representing the trigonometric functions and it's properties easier

Practice Problems
  • Convert $32^\circ$ to radians (Use a calculator)

    $$\begin{align} 32 \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} \approx 0.558505 \text{ rad} \end{align}$$

  • Convert $13 \text{ rad}$ to degrees (Use a calculator)

    $$\begin{align} 13 \cdot \frac{180}{\pi} \approx 744.845^\circ \end{align}$$

  • How many radians and degrees are in one full turn of a circle

    $2\pi$ radians or $360^\circ$ degrees

  • Convert $180^\circ$ to radians (without a calculator or the circle above)

    $180^\circ$ means we are going half way around the circle

    Since a full circle is $2\pi$ radians, half would only be $\pi$ radians

  • If we had an angle of $\frac{4\pi}{5} \text{ rad}$, approximately how far around the circle would that be (what quadrant)

    Since a half circle is $\pi \text{ rad}$ and a quarter circle is $\pi \text{rad}$

    And since $\frac{4\pi}{5}$ is in between $\frac{\pi}{2}$ and $\pi$, we went a bit before the half-way point of the circle

    This places us in the 2nd quadrant
