Trigonometry is the subject of math that deals with angles and triangles

The trigonometric functions are sine, cosine, tangent, secant, cosecant, and cotangent

The following functions use $\theta$ (theta, pronounced thay-tuh) as the angle (in radians by default or degrees)

$$\begin{align} \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} &\ \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \\ \csc(\theta) = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}} &\ \sec(\theta) = \frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}} \\ \tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} &\ \cot(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}} \\ \end{align}$$

What Do These Functions Mean?

The core idea behind trigonometric functions is that if you have a right triangle, there is an angle $\theta$

At a specific angle $\theta$ the ratios between the 3 sides of the triangles are fixed (and dependent on $\theta$)

The values of each side might be different for the same angle on differnet triangles, but the ratio between the sides is fixed

For example: look the following triangles

The angle $\theta \approx 36.86989^\circ$ is the same, but the side lengths are different

However, if we take the ratios of the side lengths it's the same throughout

$$\begin{align} \frac{5}{3} = 1.66 &\ \frac{1}{.6} = 1.66 \\ \frac{5}{4} = 1.25 &\ \frac{1}{.8} = 1.25 \\ \frac{4}{3} = 1.33 &\ \frac{.8}{.6} = 1.33 \\ \end{align}$$

This shows the ratios remain fixed for the angles


The trigonometric functions relate the ratios between the hypotenuse, opposite, and adjacent sides

The hypotenuse is the longest side

The adjacent side is the side touching the $\theta$ that's not the hypotenuse

The opposite side is the side on the on the other side of the triangle, or where the $\theta$ is pointing to

The input to the functions is an angle $\theta$, which if not specified, is in radians, in order for it to be in degrees it needs the degrees sign $^\circ$

How Do We Use These Functions?

One of the biggest uses for the trig functions is to find an unknown side

Using just trig functions alone it's possible to get all the side lengths of a right triangle with only the angle and one other side

Since they take an angle input and give a ratio, they output a constant value, but it's tricky to get exact values, so we will use a calculator

IMPORTANT: Make sure you are on either degrees or radians mode depending on your problem

If you are on the wrong setting, your answer will be completely wrong, for example:

$$\begin{align} \cos(20^\circ) \approx 0.9396 &&\text{Degrees Mode}\\ \cos(20) \approx 0.40808 &&\text{Radians Mode}\\ \end{align}$$

Most calculators today have sine, cosine, and tangent, but not the other functions which is fine because it's covered later how to compute those

Let's try to solve an example

We are given the angle as $40^\circ$ and the opposite side 12

We are trying to find the sides $h$ and $a$

First lets focus on $a$

The functions that have the ratio involving adjacent and opposite are $\tan$ and $\cot$, both of these can be used but I will use $\tan$ because it makes the algebra easier

$$\begin{align} \tan(40^\circ) = \frac{a}{12} \rightarrow 12 \cdot tan(40^\circ) = a \end{align}$$

We will have to use a calculator for this

$$\begin{align} tan(40^\circ) \approx 0.83909 \\ 12 \cdot 0.83909 = a \\ a \approx 10.069\\ \end{align}$$

This means our adjacent side length is around $10.069$

Now to find the hypotenuse, we could use the pythagorean theorem, or trig functions

$$\begin{align} \csc(40^\circ) = \frac{h}{12} \rightarrow 12 \cdot csc(40^\circ) = h \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} csc(40^\circ) \approx 1.55572 \\ 12 \cdot 1.55572 = h \\ h \approx 18.6686 \end{align}$$

So this finally gives us a right triangle with side lengths of $12,10.069,18.6686$

Memorization

These 6 functions may be daunting to remember, but there are some tricks involved

The first trick is SOH-CAH-TOA

Sine

Opposite

Hypotenuse


Cosine

Adjacent

Hypotenuse


Tangent

Opposite

Adjacent


SOH-CAH-TOA is used by splitting into the 3 parts, where the first character is the function, and the 2 characters after are the ratio

Another thing you can do is just memorize sine and cosine, because all the other functions are built off of them

$$\begin{align} \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}} &\ \cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} \\ \csc(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sin(\theta)} &\ \sec(\theta) = \frac{1}{\cos(\theta)} \\ \tan(\theta) = \frac{\sin(\theta)}{\cos(\theta)} &\ \cot(\theta) = \frac{\cos(\theta)}{\sin(\theta)} \\ \end{align}$$

All 4 of these can be found using some algebra and subsitution

Granted you will need to know the name of the functions, but that's easier then the whole thing

Practice Problems
  • Use a calculator to solve $\sin(\pi)$ (Make sure it's on radians mode)

    $$\begin{align} \sin(\pi) = 0 \end{align}$$

  • Use a calculator to solve $\cos(12^\circ)$ (Make sure it's on degree mode)

    $$\begin{align} \cos(12^\circ) \approx 0.9781 \end{align}$$

  • Use a calculator to solve $\sec(1)$ (Make sure it's on radians mode)

    $$\begin{align} \sec(1) \approx 1.8508 \end{align}$$

  • Use a calculator to solve $\tan(\pi/2)$ (Make sure it's on radians mode)

    $$\begin{align} \tan(\pi/2) \text{ is undefined} \end{align}$$

    If you want to know why it's undefined, it's because $\pi/2$ is a 90 degree angle

    Since this is a right triangle that means there is no hypotenuse if $\theta = 90^\circ$

  • What triangle does $\sin(\theta) = 12 / 20$ represent?

    $$\begin{align} \sin(\theta) = \frac{12}{20} \end{align}$$

    This is a right triangle with an opposite length of 12 and hypotenuse of 20

  • What triangle does $\tan(\theta) = 6 / 7$ represent?

    $$\begin{align} \tan(\theta) = \frac{6}{7} \end{align}$$

    This is a right triangle with an opposite length of 7 and adjacent of 7

  • Find the the hypotenuse of a right triangle where an angle is $\pi/6$ and the opposite side is $12$

    $$\begin{align} \csc(\pi/6) = \frac{h}{12} \\ 12\csc(\pi/6) = h \\ h = 24 \end{align}$$

  • If a 15-ft ladder is hanging at $63^\circ$ angle, how high can it reach? (The ladder leaning makes a right angle against the object it's leaning on)

    Since the ladder is 15-ft, that's the length of the ladder, so it's the hypotenuse

    We are trying to find how high it can reach against the object it's leaning it, so we are trying to find the opposite side

    $$\begin{align} \sin(63^\circ) = \frac{o}{15} \\ 15\sin(63^\circ) = o \\ o \approx 13.3650 \end{align}$$

    The ladder can reach around 13.365-ft
