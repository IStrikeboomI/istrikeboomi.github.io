Like the regular trigonometric functions, which take in an angle and output a ratio

The inverse trigonometric functions take in a ratio and output an angle

The inverse trigonometric functions are:

$$\begin{align} \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\right) = \theta &\ \cos^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\right) = \theta \\ \csc^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}}\right) = \theta &\ \sec^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}}\right) = \theta \\ \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} \right) = \theta &\ \cot^{-1}\left(\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}} \right) = \theta \\ \end{align}$$

There is some ambiguity to whether the $-1$ is an exponent or denoting the inverse

To remedy this, there is an alternate notation where you put arc- in front of the function

$$\begin{align} \arcsin\left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\right) = \theta &\ \arccos\left(\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}}\right) = \theta \\ arccsc\left(\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{opposite}}\right) = \theta &\ arcsec\left(\frac{\text{hypotenuse}}{\text{adjacent}}\right) = \theta \\ \arctan\left(\frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{adjacent}} \right) = \theta &\ arccot\left(\frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{opposite}} \right) = \theta \\ \end{align}$$

Both of these notations are widely accepeted and represent the same thing

When trying to find the answers, you might need a calculator, make sure that it is either degrees or radians mode

A lot of calculators might not have $arccsc,arcsec,arccot$

If you want to use these functions, you can use plug $1/x$ into $\arcsin,\arccos,\arctan$ where $x$ is your input

Simplifying Inverse Trigonometric Expressions

Before I start, assume we are talking about triangles that actually can exist

There are some domain and range issues which we will cover in the unit circle section

Anyways, simplifying inverse trig expressions is the process of simplifying a nested inverse trig inside a regular trig

For example:

$$\begin{align} \sec(\tan^{-1}(x)) \end{align}$$

This can be simplified further using right triangles

The $\sec$ takes in a $\theta$, and the $\tan^{-1}$ outputs a $\theta$

This means they can be used to make a triangle

$$\begin{align} \tan^{-1}(x) = \theta \rightarrow \tan(\theta) = \frac{x}{1} \end{align}$$

Since $\tan(\theta) = \frac{opposite}{adjacent}$, that means we have a right triangle with opposite of $x$ and adjacent of $1$

We can use the pythagorean theorem ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$) to find the hypotenuse

$$\begin{align} a^2 + b^2 &= c^2 \\ x^2 + 1^2 &= c^2 \\ c &= \sqrt{x^2 + 1} \end{align}$$

Since $\sec(\theta) = \frac{hypotenuse}{adjacent}$,

$$\begin{align} \sec(\theta) = \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}}{1} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \sec(tan^{-1}(x))= \sqrt{x^2 + 1} \end{align}$$

Another example:

$$\begin{align} \sin(\tan^{-1}(x)) \end{align}$$

What we first do is try to recognize what $\tan^{-1}(x)$ means

If we rewrite $\tan^{-1}(x)$ into $\tan^{-1}(\frac{x}{1})$ we have a ratio inside the inverse tangent

That ratio gives us a right triangle with opposite of x and an adjacent of 1

Using the pythagorean theorem we can complete this triangle with a hypotenuse of $\sqrt{x^2 + 1}$

Since we have the hypotenuse and the opposite, thats all we need for sine (which is opposite over hypotenuse)

$$\begin{align} \sin(\tan^{-1}(x)) = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}} \end{align}$$

Lets do another example

$$\begin{align} \tan(\arccos(2x)) \end{align}$$

The ratio inside the inverse cosine gives us an adjacent of 2x and hypotenuse of 1

Using pythagorean theorem we get an opposite of $\sqrt{1-4x^2}$

Using tangent is opposite over adjacent

$$\begin{align} \tan(\arccos(2x)) = \frac{\sqrt{1-4x^2}}{2x} \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Find $\sin^{-1}(1/2)$ in degrees (use a calculator)

    $$\begin{align} sin^{-1}(1/2) = 30^\circ \end{align}$$

  • Find $\cos^{-1}(4/5)$ in radians (use a calculator)

    $$\begin{align} cos^{-1}(4/5) \approx 0.64350 \text{ rad} \end{align}$$

  • Find $\tan^{-1}(2)$ in radians (use a calculator)

    $$\begin{align} tan^{-1}(2) \approx 1.107148 \text{ rad} \end{align}$$

  • What kind of triangle would $arcsec(12 / 3)$ represent (give all side lengths)

    $arcsec\left(\frac{12}{3}\right)$ give a triangle with hypotenuse of 12 and adjacent of 3

    To find opposite we use pythagorean theorem $\sqrt{12^2 - 3^2} = \sqrt{135} = 3\sqrt{15}$

  • Simplify $\cos(\cos^{-1}{x})$

    Since $\cos$ and $\cos^{-1}$ are inverses they just equal

    $$\begin{align} \cos(\cos^{-1}{x}) = x \end{align}$$

  • Solve $\sin(\cos^{-1}{(4/5)})$

    Since cosine is adjacent over hypotenuse, we have a triangle with adjacent of 4 and hypotenuse of 5

    The opposite of that would be $\sqrt{5^2 - 4^2} = \sqrt{9} = 3$

    $$\begin{align} \sin(\cos^{-1}{\frac{4}{5}}) = \frac{3}{5} \end{align}$$

  • Solve $\cos(\sin^{-1}(x/3))$

    Since opposite is x and hypotenuse is 3, adjacent is $\sqrt{9 - x^2}$

    $$\begin{align} \cos\left(\sin^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{3}\right)\right) = \frac{\sqrt{9 - x^2}}{3} \end{align}$$
