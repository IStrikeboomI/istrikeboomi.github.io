The cross product the 3 dimensional vectors $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ is :

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} = \left< u_2v_3 - u_3v_2, u_3v_1 - u_1v_3,u_1v_2 - u_2v_1 \right> \end{align}$$

Cross product is only defined for 3 dimensional vectors

Determinants

Before we talk about what the cross product can be interpreted as, we should talk about determinants

If you recall from the matrix section, this is the same determinant

The determinat of a $2 \times 2$ matrix is

$$\begin{align} \det\left( \begin{bmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{bmatrix}\right) = \begin{vmatrix} a & b\\ c & d \end{vmatrix} = ad - bc \end{align}$$

The determinant of $3 \times 3$ matrix is:

$$\begin{align} \begin{vmatrix} a & b & c\\ e & f & g \\ h & i & j \end{vmatrix} &= a \begin{vmatrix} f & g \\ i & j \end{vmatrix} - b \begin{vmatrix} e & g \\ h & j \end{vmatrix} + c \begin{vmatrix} e & f \\ h & i \end{vmatrix} \\ &= a(fj - gi) - b(ej - gh) + c(ei - fh) \end{align}$$

These coefficients can be remembered by the following matrixes

$$\begin{align} \begin{vmatrix} a & & \\ & f & g \\ & i & j \end{vmatrix} \begin{vmatrix} & b & \\ e& & g \\ h& & j \end{vmatrix} \begin{vmatrix} & & c\\ e& f & \\ h& i & \end{vmatrix} \end{align}$$

This is all relevant because the cross product can be thought of as a determinant

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} &= \begin{vmatrix} \textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \begin{vmatrix} u_2 & u_3 \\ v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix} \textbf{i} - \begin{vmatrix} u_1 & u_3 \\ v_1 & v_3 \end{vmatrix} \textbf{j} + \begin{vmatrix} u_1 & u_2 \\ v_1 & v_2 \end{vmatrix} \textbf{k} \end{align}$$

Properties

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \times \vec{v} = -(\vec{v} \times \vec{u}) \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \times (\vec{v} + \vec{w}) = (\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) + (\vec{u} \times \vec{w}) \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} c(\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) = c\vec{u} \times \vec{v} = \vec{u} \times c\vec{v} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \times \vec{0} = \vec{0} \times \vec{v} = \vec{0} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \times \vec{u} = \vec{0} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \cdot (\vec{v} \times \vec{w}) = \vec{v} \cdot (\vec{w} \times \vec{u}) = \vec{w} \cdot (\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} ||\vec{u} \times \vec{v}|| = ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sin(\theta) \end{align}$$

What Does This Even Mean?

The core idea behind the cross product is that it results in a vector that is orthogonal (perpendicular) to both vectors

Here's the same graph at other angles

So why does doing cross product create an orthogonal vector? Let's do a proof

Recalling from dot product, if the dot product is 0 it means the vectors are orthogonal

The dot products of $\vec{u} \times \vec{v}$ to both vectors should be $0$

$$\begin{align} \vec{u} \cdot (\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) &= \vec{u} \cdot \left< u_2v_3 - u_3v_2, u_3v_1 - u_1v_3,u_1v_2 - u_2v_1 \right> \\ &= u_1(u_2v_3 - u_3v_2) + u_2(u_3v_1 - u_1v_3) + u_3(u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)\\ &= \textcolor{red}{u_1u_2v_3} - \textcolor{blue}{u_1u_3v_2} + \textcolor{green}{u_2u_3v_1} -\textcolor{red}{u_2u_1v_3} + \textcolor{blue}{u_3u_1v_2} - \textcolor{green}{u_3u_2v_1}\\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

And similarly for $\vec{v}$

$$\begin{align} \vec{v} \cdot (\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) &= \vec{v} \cdot \left< u_2v_3 - u_3v_2, u_3v_1 - u_1v_3,u_1v_2 - u_2v_1 \right> \\ &= v_1(u_2v_3 - u_3v_2) + v_2(u_3v_1 - u_1v_3) + v_3(u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)\\ &= \textcolor{red}{v_1u_2v_3} - \textcolor{blue}{v_1u_3v_2} + \textcolor{green}{v_2u_3v_1} -\textcolor{blue}{v_2u_1v_3} + \textcolor{green}{v_3u_1v_2} - \textcolor{red}{v_3u_2v_1}\\ &= 0 \end{align}$$

Why Sine?

In order to prove $ ||\vec{u} \times \vec{v}|| = ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sin(\theta)$, we can use algebra

First, establish definition of cosine between two vectors

$$\begin{align} cos(\theta) &= \frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{||\vec{u}||\cdot ||\vec{v}||} \end{align}$$

$$\begin{align} ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sin(\theta) &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sqrt{1-\cos^2(\theta)} &&\text{Using Rearranged $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$} \\ &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sqrt{1-\left(\frac{\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v}}{||\vec{u}||\cdot ||\vec{v}||}\right)^2} \\ &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sqrt{1-\frac{(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v})^2}{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2}} \\ &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sqrt{\frac{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2}{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2}-\frac{(\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v})^2}{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2}} \\ &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \sqrt{\frac{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2 - (\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v})^2}{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2}} \\ &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot \frac{1}{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2} \cdot \sqrt{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2 - (\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v})^2} &&\text{Factor Out Denominator Out Of Square Root}\\ &= \sqrt{||\vec{u}||^2\cdot ||\vec{v}||^2 - (\vec{u}\cdot\vec{v})^2} &&\text{Cancel Out Terms}\\ &= \sqrt{(u_1^2 + u_2^2 + u_3^2)(v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2) - (u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3)^2}\\ &= \sqrt{(u_2v_3 - u_3v_2)^2 + (u_3v_1 - u_1v_3)^2 + (u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)^2} &&\text{A Lot Of Algebra Later...}\\ &= ||\vec{u} \times \vec{v}||\\ \end{align}$$

Area Of A Parallelogram

The Area of a Parallelogram with the sides of $\vec{u}$ and $\vec{v}$ is $||\vec{u} \times \vec{v}||$

First recall the area of a parallelogram formula

$$\begin{align} A = h \times l \end{align}$$

The area of the above parallelogram would be

$$\begin{align} A &= ||\vec{u}|| \cdot ||\vec{v}|| \cdot sin(\theta) \\ &= ||\vec{u} \times \vec{v}|| \end{align}$$

Now you might notice that if you have a 2-D parallelogram, you can't do cross product because it's only in 3-D

What you would do is to make your vector into a 3-D vector with the $Z$ or $\textbf{k}$ component to 0

For Example:

$$\begin{align} \left<2,5 \right> \rightarrow \left< 2,5,0 \right> \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Find $\vec{a} \times \vec{b}$ where $\vec{a} = \left< 3, 3, -1 \right>$ and $\vec{b} =\left< 4, 9, 2 \right>$

    $$\begin{align} \vec{a} &= \left< 3, 3, -1 \right> \\ \vec{b} &= \left< 4, 9, 2 \right> \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} &= \begin{vmatrix} \textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ 3 & 3 & -1 \\ 4 & 9 & 2 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \left< -15, -2, 39 \right> \end{align}$$

  • Find the area of a parallelogram with vertices $A(1,6)$,$B(3,9)$, and $C(6,6)$ using cross products

    We can create two vectors both with the starting point of $A$ and one goes to $b$, and the other goes to $C$

    $$\begin{align} \vec{AB} &= \left< 3-1,9-6,0 \right> = \left< 2,3,0 \right> \\ \vec{AC} &= \left< 6-1,6-6,0 \right> = \left< 5,0,0 \right> \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \vec{AB} \times \vec{AC} &= \begin{vmatrix} \textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ 2 & 3 & 0 \\ 5 & 0 & 0 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \left< 0,0,15 \right> \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} ||\vec{AB} \times \vec{AC}|| = \sqrt{0 + 0 + 15^2} = 15 \end{align}$$

  • Find the area of a parallelogram with side vectors of $\vec{a} = \left< 2,4,2 \right>$ and $\vec{b} = \left< 4,9,5 \right>$

    $$\begin{align} \vec{a} \times \vec{b} &= \begin{vmatrix} \textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ 2 & 4 & 2 \\ 4 & 9 & 5 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \left< 2,-2,2 \right> \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} ||\vec{a} \times \vec{b}|| = \sqrt{2^2 + (-2)^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{12} = 2\sqrt{3} \end{align}$$
