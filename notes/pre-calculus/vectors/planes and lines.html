Vectors make 3D graphing easier, first we need to establish some vocabulary

If you want to make 3D graphs, I like to use Desmos

Planes

A plane can be thought of as the 3D extension of a 2D line

It is a flat 2D surface that extends indefinitely

Below is the plane $2x + y - 2z - 3 = 0$, with the normal vector shown at different angles

A plane containing the point $P(x_1,y_1,z_1)$, with a normal vector of $\vec{n} = \left< a,b,c \right>$ is:

$$\begin{align} a(x-x_1) + b(y-y_1) + z(z-z_1) = 0 \end{align}$$

This can be written more generally (where $d = -ax_1 -by_1 -cz_1$),

$$\begin{align} ax + by + cz + d = 0 \end{align}$$

The normal vector $\vec{n}$ of a plane is a vector that is orthogonal to the plane

The reason why planes are defined using the normal vector can be proven by the following

First define $Q(x,y,z)$ as a point on the plane, and make the vector $\vec{PQ}$

$$\begin{align} \vec{PQ} = \left< x-x_1,y-y_1,z-z_1 \right> \end{align}$$

Since $\vec{PQ}$ lies on the plane (because both points are on the plane), and $\vec{n}$ is orthogonal, that means the Dot Product is zero

$$\begin{align} \vec{n} \cdot \vec{PQ} = 0 \rightarrow a(x-x_1) + b(y-y_1) + c(z-z_1) = 0 \end{align}$$

To find the angle between two planes, the angle between the planes would reflect throughout the whole plane

This includes the normal vectors

$$\begin{align} \cos(\theta) = \frac{|\vec{n_1} \cdot \vec{n_2} |}{||\vec{n_1}||\cdot||\vec{n_2}||} \end{align}$$

The distance between a point $Q(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and a plane $ax + by + cz + d = 0$ is:

$$\begin{align} D = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}} \end{align}$$

The way we prove is this is to first define a point on the plane $P(x_2,y_2,z_2)$

We need to find the distance from $Q$ to the plane, we can map the distance onto the normal vector using a projection onto n

$$\begin{align} \vec{PQ} &= \left< x_2 - x_1, y_2 - y_1, z_2 - z_1 \right> \\ D = |proj_{\vec{n}}{\vec{PQ}}| &= \frac{|\vec{PQ} \cdot \vec{n}|}{||\vec{n}||} = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}} \end{align}$$

Lines

Traditional lines also exist in the 3d plane, but they have to be written parametrically

The equation of a line parallel to $\vec{v} = \left< a,b,c\right>$ and passing through the point $P(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ is:

$$\begin{align} x &= x_1 + at \\ y &= y_1 + bt \\ z &= z_1 + ct \\ \end{align}$$

The line can also be written in symmetric form,

$$\begin{align} \frac{x - x_1}{a} = \frac{y - y_1}{b} = \frac{z -z_1}{c} \end{align}$$

Below is the line $x = 1 + t, y = 2 - \frac{1}{2}t, 1 + t$ at different angles

The distance between a point $Q$ and a point on the line $P$ and a direction vector $\vec{u}$ is

$$\begin{align} D = \frac{||\vec{PQ} \times \vec{u}||}{||\vec{u}||} \end{align}$$

The direction vector can be a vector with just the compoennts as the parametric parts of the line

This can be proved by the fact that a right triangle is formed between $P$ and $Q$

$$\begin{align} D = ||\vec{PQ}||\sin(\theta) = \frac{||\vec{PQ}||\cdot ||\vec{u}|| \cdot \sin(\theta)}{||\vec{u}|| } = \frac{||\vec{PQ} \times \vec{u}||}{||\vec{u}||} \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Find the plane containing the points $A(3,1,-4), B(2,0,-3), C(5,1,0)$

    The way we achieve this is to create the vectors $\vec{AB}$ and $\vec{AC}$

    We then find the normal vector by finding the vector that is orthogonal, by using the cross product

    $$\begin{align} \vec{AB} = \left< -1,-1,1 \right> \\ \vec{AC} = \left< 2,0,4 \right> \\ \vec{n} = \vec{AB} \times \vec{AC} &= \begin{vmatrix} \textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ -1 & -1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 4 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \left< -4,6,2 \right> \end{align}$$

    Now that we have the normal vector, we choose any of the three points as a refernce and plug it in to the plane formula

    I'm going to use point $A$

    $$\begin{align} a(x-x_1) + b(y-y_1) + z(z-z_1) = 0 \\ -4(x-3) + 6(y-1) + 2(z+4) = 0 \\ -4x + 12 + 6y -6 + 2z + 8 = 0 \\ -4x+ 6y + 2z + 14 = 0 \\ 2x - 3y - z - 7 = 0 \end{align}$$

  • Find the point of intersection of the line $\frac{x + 3}{5} = \frac{y-2}{3} = \frac{z + 1}{2}$ and the plane $3x - 2y + z = 8$

    $$\begin{align} \frac{x + 3}{5} = \frac{y-2}{3} = \frac{z + 1}{2} \\ 3x - 2y + z = 8 \end{align}$$

    What we are going to do is convert the line into parametric form so we can isolate $x,y,z$

    We can then plug these into the plane to get the value of $t$, then plug that back into the line to get the point

    $$\begin{align} x &= -3 + 5t \\ y &= 2 + 3t \\ z &= -1 + 2t \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} 3(-3 + 5t) - 2(2 + 3t) + (-1 + 2t) = 8 \\ 11t = 22 \\ t = 2 \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} x &= -3 + 5(2) = 7 \\ y &= 2 + 3(2) = 8 \\ z &= -1 + 2(2) = 3\\ \end{align}$$

    This gives the point of $(7,8,3)$

  • Find the cosine of the angle between the planes $3x - 2y + z = 7$ and $5x − y − 2z = 2$

    We need to use the plane cosine formula

    First we need to find the normal vectors (which are the coefficients)

    $$\begin{align} \vec{n_1} = \left< 3,-2,1 \right> \\ \vec{n_2} = \left< 5,-1,-2 \right> \\ \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \cos(\theta) &= \frac{|\vec{n_1} \cdot \vec{n_2}|}{||\vec{n_1}||\cdot||\vec{n_2}||} \\ &= \frac{|15 + 2 - 2|}{\sqrt{3^2 + (-2)^2 + 1^2} \sqrt{5^2 + (-1)^2 + (-2)^2}} \\ &= \frac{15}{\sqrt{14} \sqrt{30}} \\ &= \frac{15}{\sqrt{7 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 15}} \\ &= \frac{15}{2\sqrt{105}} \\ \end{align}$$

  • Find the distance between $(6,1,0)$ from the plane that is perpendicular to $\textbf{i} -2\textbf{j} + \textbf{k}$

    The vector that is perpendicular to the plane is the normal vector, we can the plane from that

    $$\begin{align} \vec{n} = \left< 1,-2,1 \right> \\ x - 2y + z = 0 \end{align}$$

    Now we can use the distance formula

    $$\begin{align} D &= \frac{|1\cdot6 + -2\cdot1 + 1\cdot0 + 0|}{\sqrt{1^2 + (-2)^2 + 1^2}} \\ &= \frac{|4|}{\sqrt{6}} \\ &= \frac{4}{\sqrt{6}} \\ \end{align}$$

  • Find the distance between the point $A(3,-2,-3)$ and the line through the points $B(2,-1,3)$ and $C(3,0,-2)$

    We don't need to the find the line because for the distance formula, we just need a point on the line and a direction vector

    The direction vector is $\vec{BC}$, because they are both on the line

    $$\begin{align} \vec{u} = \vec{BC} = \left< 1,1,-5 \right> \\ \vec{BA} = \left< 1,-1,-6 \right> \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} \vec{BA} \times \vec{u} &= \begin{vmatrix} \textbf{i} & \textbf{j} & \textbf{k}\\ 1 & -1 & -6 \\ 1 & 1 & -5 \end{vmatrix} \\ &= \left< 11, -1, 2 \right> \end{align}$$

    $$\begin{align} D &= \frac{\sqrt{11^2 + 1^2 + 2^2}}{\sqrt{1^2 + 1^2 + 5^2}} \\ &= \frac{\sqrt{126}}{\sqrt{27}} \\ &= \sqrt{\frac{126}{27}} \\ &= \sqrt{\frac{14}{3}} \\ \end{align}$$
