An imaginary number is a real number multiplied by the imaginary unit \(i\)

Here $i^2 = -1$ or $i = \sqrt{-1}$

The addition of a real number and an imaginary number is called a complex number.

A complex number is in the form of $a + bi$, where $a,b \in \mathbb{R}$ (real numbers), and $i = \sqrt{-1}$

This is called cartesian form or rectangular form

The set of all imaginary numbers is denoted by $\mathbb{I}$

The set of all complex numbers is denoted by $\mathbb{C}$

All real numbers and imaginary numbers are subsets of complex numbers

Instead of using $x$, we typically use $z$ to represent known complex numbers.

What are Imaginary Numbers?

An imaginary number is one where when you square it, the result is a negative real number.

This is needed because the square root of a negative number is not defined in the set of real numbers.

Negative square roots can appear when solving equations like $x^2 + 1 = 0$

To solve this, we define the imaginary unit \(i\) such that \(i^2 = -1\)

Thus, the square root of any negative number can be expressed as a multiple of \(i\)

For example, $\sqrt{-9} = \sqrt{9} \cdot \sqrt{-1} = 3i$

Polynomial Example

Consider the polynomial equation $x^2 + 4 = 0$

To solve for \(x\), we can rearrange the equation:

$$\begin{align} x^2 + 4 &= 0 \\ x^2 &= -4 \\ x &= \pm \sqrt{-4} \\ x &= \pm 2i \end{align}$$


For another polynomial consider $x^2 + 3x + 12 = 0$

We can use the quadratic formula to solve for \(x\):

$$\begin{align} x &= \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \\ a &= 1, b = 3, c = 12 \\ x &= \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{3^2 - 4(1)(12)}}{2(1)} \\ x &= \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{9 - 48}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{-39}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{39}i}{2} \\ x &= \frac{-3}{2} \pm \frac{\sqrt{39}}{2}i \end{align}$$

Properties of Complex Numbers

Complex numbers can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided using the same rules as real numbers

Just remember \(i^2 = -1\)

For example, to add two complex numbers:

$$\begin{align} (2 + 3i) + (4 + 5i) &= (2 + 4) + (3i + 5i) \\ &= 6 + 8i \end{align}$$

To multiply two complex numbers (distribute each term):

$$\begin{align} (1 + 2i)(3 + 4i) &= 1 \cdot 3 + 1 \cdot 4i + 2i \cdot 3 + 2i \cdot 4i \\ &= 3 + 4i + 6i + 8i^2 \\ &= 3 + 10i + 8(-1) \\ &= -5 + 10i \end{align}$$

Conjugate of a Complex Number

The conjugate of a complex number is obtained by changing the sign of the imaginary part.

For example, the conjugate of \(a + bi\) is \(a - bi\)

The conjugate of $z$ is denoted as \(\overline{z}\) or \(z^*\)

Conjugates are useful in division of complex numbers to eliminate the imaginary part from the denominator.

For example, to divide two complex numbers:

$$\begin{align} \frac{2 + 3i}{1 + 4i} &= \frac{(2 + 3i)(1 - 4i)}{(1 + 4i)(1 - 4i)} \\ &= \frac{2 - 8i + 3i - 12i^2}{1 - 16i^2} \\ &= \frac{2 - 5i + 12}{1 + 16} \\ &= \frac{14 - 5i}{17} \\ &= \frac{14}{17} - \frac{5}{17}i \end{align}$$

Real and Imaginary Operators

For a complex number $$z = a + bi$$

The real operator $\Re(z)$ extract the real part $\Re(z) = a$ (Also denoted as $\text{Re}(z)$)

The imaginary operator $\Im(z)$ extract the imaginary part $\Im(z) = b$ (Also denoted as $\text{Im}(z)$)

Complex Plane

The complex plane is a 2d extension of the number line to represent complex numbers graphically.

The horizontal axis (x-axis) represents the real part of the complex number.

The vertical axis (y-axis) represents the imaginary part of the complex number.

For example, the complex number \(3 + 2i\) is represented by the point (3, 2) in the complex plane.

Multiplying by $i$

When you multiply by $i$, you rotate by $90^\circ$ (or \(\frac{\pi}{2}\) radians) counterclockwise in the complex plane.

Expressed numerically

$$\begin{align} 1 \cdot i &= i \quad & (0^\circ \to 90^\circ) \\ i \cdot i &= i^2 = -1 \quad & (90^\circ \to 180^\circ) \\ -1 \cdot i &= -i \quad & (180^\circ \to 270^\circ) \\ -i \cdot i &= -i^2 = 1 \quad & (270^\circ \to 360^\circ) \end{align}$$

This cycle repeats every four multiplications by $i$

$$\begin{align} i^0 &= 1 \\ i^1 &= i \\ i^2 &= -1 \\ i^3 &= -i \\ i^4 &= 1 \\ &\vdots \\ i^{n} = 1 &&\text{Where } n \mod 4 = 0 \\ i^{n+1} = i \\ i^{n+2} = -1 \\ i^{n+3} = -i \\ i^{n+4} = 1 \\ \end{align}$$

Practice Problems
  • Given $z = 4i$, find the conjugate $z^*$

    $$z^* = -4i$$

  • Compute $z = (4+2i)(i + 1) - (3+2i)$, and find $\Re(z)$ and $\Im(z)$

    $$\begin{align} z &= (4+2i)(i + 1) - (3+2i) \\ &= 4i + 4 + 2i^2 + 2i - 3 - 2i \\ &= 4i + 4 - 2 + 2i - 3 - 2i \\ &= (4 - 2 - 3) + (4i + 2i - 2i) \\ &= -1 + 4i \\ \Re(z) &= -1 \\ \Im(z) &= 4 \end{align}$$

  • Solve for $x$: $x^2 + 2x + 5 = 0$

    $$\begin{align} x &= \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \\ a &= 1, b = 2, c = 5 \\ x &= \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{2^2 - 4(1)(5)}}{2(1)} \\ x &= \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 20}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{-2 \pm 4i}{2} \\ x &= -1 \pm 2i \end{align}$$

  • Compute $i^{2587}$

    Instead of multiplying 2587 times, use the fact that powers of $i$ repeat every 4 steps.

    $2587 / 4 = 646$ remainder $3$

    Thus, $i^{2587} = i^3 = -i$

  • Compute $z = \frac{2-2i}{4 + i}$, and find $\Re(z)$ and $\Im(z)$

    $$\begin{align} z &= \frac{2-2i}{4 + i} \\ &= \frac{(2-2i)(4 - i)}{(4 + i)(4 - i)} \\ &= \frac{8 - 2i - 8i + 2i^2}{16 - i^2} \\ &= \frac{8 - 10i + 2(-1)}{16 + 1} \\ &= \frac{6 - 10i}{17} \\ &= \frac{6}{17} - \frac{10}{17}i \\ \Re(z) &= \frac{6}{17} \\ \Im(z) &= -\frac{10}{17} \end{align}$$
